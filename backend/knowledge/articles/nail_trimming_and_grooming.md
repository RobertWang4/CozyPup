## Why it matters
Regular grooming is not just cosmetic. It keeps the coat and skin healthy, prevents painful mats and overgrown nails, and gives you a chance to find lumps, wounds, parasites, or ear problems early. Most dogs and cats need at minimum: **nail trims**, **brushing**, and **occasional bathing**.

## Nail trimming
**How often.** Most pets need their nails trimmed roughly every **3–4 weeks**. Dogs who walk regularly on pavement may wear the nails down naturally and need trims less often; indoor-only dogs, cats, and seniors usually need trims more often. A useful rule: if you **hear the nails clicking** on a hard floor, or if they touch the ground when the pet stands still, they are too long.

**Finding the quick.** The **quick** is the pink blood vessel and nerve that runs down the center of the nail. Cutting into it hurts the pet and causes bleeding.
- **Light-colored nails:** you can see the pink quick through the nail. Trim **just in front of** the pink, taking only the clear tip.
- **Dark-colored nails:** you cannot see the quick. Take **very small slices** at a time. Each slice exposes a fresh cross-section; stop as soon as you see a **small dark dot in the center** — that is the edge of the quick. One more slice would cut it.

**Clippers vs. grinders.**
- **Scissor-style or guillotine clippers** are fast and work well if they are sharp and the right size for the pet.
- **Rotary grinders (Dremel-style)** round the nail smoothly and make it easier to avoid the quick, but noise and vibration take getting used to. Introduce slowly with treats.
- Either tool is fine — pick what you and your pet tolerate best. **Dull clippers crush the nail**, which hurts; replace or sharpen them.

**If you cut too short (cut the quick).**
- **Stay calm.** It looks dramatic but is rarely dangerous.
- Apply **styptic powder** (sold at pet stores) to the bleeding tip with gentle pressure for 30–60 seconds. In a pinch, cornstarch or flour can slow bleeding.
- Keep the pet quiet and off carpet until bleeding stops. If bleeding doesn't stop within about 20 minutes, or the paw becomes swollen or painful over the next day, call the vet.
- Give a treat and end the session on a positive note — your pet will remember.

## Brushing
Brushing removes loose hair, prevents mats, and spreads natural skin oils.
- **Short-coated dogs and cats:** once a week is usually enough.
- **Long or double-coated pets:** several times a week, ideally daily during heavy shedding seasons.
- Use the right tool: a slicker brush and metal comb for long coats, a rubber curry or bristle brush for short coats, a de-shedding tool for heavy undercoats.
- **Do not cut out mats with scissors** — it is very easy to cut the skin underneath. Work mats out with a comb and detangling spray, or have a groomer or vet shave them out with clippers made for pets.

## Bathing
- **Dogs:** about **once a month** is a reasonable baseline, or whenever the coat is dirty or smelly. Over-bathing strips skin oils and can cause dryness and itching. Some medical conditions call for medicated baths on a vet-recommended schedule.
- **Cats:** most cats groom themselves and **rarely need baths** unless they get into something messy, have a skin condition, or are long-haired and matting.
- Use a **shampoo made for pets**. Human shampoo, dish soap, and especially products with **tea tree oil or essential oils** can irritate skin or be toxic, particularly to cats.
- Rinse thoroughly — shampoo residue is a common cause of post-bath itching.
- Dry the ears gently; trapped moisture encourages ear infections.

## Ears, eyes, and teeth (the rest of "grooming")
- **Ears:** peek inside weekly. A little wax is normal; red, smelly, painful, or dark-brown gunky ears need a vet visit, not a deeper home cleaning.
- **Eyes:** wipe away normal tear staining with a soft damp cloth. Squinting, redness, or discharge that is green/yellow is a vet issue.
- **Teeth:** see the dental care article — brushing is the single most effective home grooming habit for long-term health.

## When to see a veterinarian or professional groomer
- Nails so long they curl into the paw pad, or a torn/bleeding nail that won't stop
- Severe matting, especially near the skin, ears, rear end, or armpits
- Any new lump, scab, hot spot, or area of hair loss you notice during grooming
- Pets who become fearful or aggressive during grooming — a vet can rule out pain, and a professional groomer or trainer can help with handling
- Senior, arthritic, or anxious pets may do better with professional grooming or sedated grooming at the vet
