## What it is
The **conjunctiva** is the thin pink membrane that covers the white of the eye and the inside of the eyelids. **Conjunctivitis** — inflammation of that membrane, commonly called "pink eye" — is one of the most frequent eye problems in dogs. It causes redness, swelling, and discharge, and can have many different causes.

## Common causes
- **Infections** — bacterial or viral, often spreading from the respiratory tract.
- **Allergies** — environmental (pollen, dust) or reactions to a new product.
- **Environmental irritants** — shampoo, chlorine, smoke, dust, wind.
- **Foreign material** — a grass seed, eyelash, or debris trapped under the lid.
- **Eyelid problems** — eyelids that turn inward (entropion) or outward (ectropion), or abnormal eyelashes rubbing the eye.
- **Tear-duct problems** — blocked drainage leaves the eye wet and irritated.
- **Dry eye (keratoconjunctivitis sicca)** — too few tears; eye looks dull and sticky.
- **Underlying systemic illness** — eye problems can be the first visible sign of a disease elsewhere in the body.

## Signs to watch for
- **Redness** of the eye or the inside of the eyelids
- **Swelling** around the eye
- **Discharge** — clear, cloudy, yellow/green, or crusty
- **Squinting, holding the eye closed, or blinking more than usual**
- **Pawing or rubbing** at the eye
- **Light sensitivity** (squinting in bright light)
- A **third eyelid** more visible than usual
- **Cloudy or blue tint** to the clear front of the eye
- A pet that keeps one eye closed entirely

## When to seek veterinary care
Eye problems can progress fast. Call a veterinarian **the same day** for any of the following, and treat sudden severe signs as an emergency:
- Sudden or severe redness, swelling, or pain
- The eye held shut, or the pet refusing to open it
- **Cloudy or bluish** appearance of the eye
- Sudden vision loss or stumbling into objects
- Injury to the eye or a suspected foreign body (grass seed, stick)
- A visible scratch, cut, or ulcer on the surface
- The eyeball **protruding** or looking like it has moved out of the socket (emergency — cover with a moist cloth and go immediately)
- Chemical splash (shampoo, cleaner, toxin) — **flush with clean water or saline for 15 minutes and go directly to the vet**

## What the vet may do
A veterinary eye exam typically includes:
- Checking vision and pupil response
- Looking at the front of the eye with magnification and a bright light
- **Fluorescein stain** to look for scratches or ulcers on the cornea
- **Tear production test** (Schirmer test) if dry eye is suspected
- Measuring **eye pressure** to rule out glaucoma
- Checking under the eyelids for foreign material
- Swab or culture for persistent or unusual discharge

Treatment depends on the cause: antibiotic drops or ointments for infection, artificial tears or specific medications for dry eye, removal of a foreign body, surgery for eyelid defects, or treatment of an underlying disease.

## Home care guidance
- **Do not use human eye drops or home remedies** (including redness-relieving products, steroid creams, or breast milk) — some can mask a serious problem or damage the eye.
- Gently wipe discharge from the outside corner using a clean, moist, lint-free cloth. Use a separate cloth for each eye.
- Prevent the dog from rubbing — an e-collar is the safest option while healing.
- Keep the dog out of dusty, smoky, or windy conditions while recovering.
- Finish the **full prescribed course** of any drops or ointments, even if the eye looks better after a couple of days.
