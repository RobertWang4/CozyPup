## What it is
The **conjunctiva** is the thin pink membrane that covers the white of the eye and the inside of the eyelids. **Conjunctivitis** — inflammation of that membrane — is common in cats and usually looks like redness, swelling, and watery or mucous discharge. In cats, conjunctivitis is often linked to a **respiratory infection** and can be contagious to other cats.

## Common causes in cats
- **Feline herpesvirus (FHV-1)** — the most common cause; often runs in multi-cat households and can flare with stress.
- **Feline calicivirus** — often causes ulcers in the mouth as well as eye and nose signs.
- **Chlamydia felis, Mycoplasma** — bacterial causes.
- **Allergies or irritants** — dust, smoke, pollen, strong cleaning products.
- **Foreign material** in the eye or under the eyelid.
- **Eyelid abnormalities** — less common in cats than dogs, but possible.
- **Dry eye** — less common in cats than in dogs but does occur.
- **Underlying systemic illness**, especially in older cats.

## Signs to watch for
- **Redness** of the eye or the inside of the eyelids
- **Swelling** around the eye
- **Discharge** — watery, cloudy, yellow/green, or crusty
- **Squinting, eye held shut, or increased blinking**
- **Rubbing at the eye**, rubbing the face on surfaces
- **Light sensitivity**
- A **third eyelid** more visible than usual
- **Cloudy or bluish** color of the clear front of the eye
- Sneezing or nasal discharge alongside eye signs (respiratory infection)

## When to seek veterinary care
Eye problems can worsen within hours. Call a veterinarian **the same day** for any of the following, and treat sudden severe signs as an emergency:
- Sudden or severe redness, swelling, or pain
- The eye held shut, or the cat refusing to open it
- **Cloudy or bluish** cornea
- Visible scratch, ulcer, or deep redness
- A foreign body suspected in the eye
- An eye that has popped out of the socket — emergency; cover with a moist cloth and go now
- Chemical splash — **flush with clean water or saline for 15 minutes and go directly to the vet**
- Sudden vision loss
- A kitten with eye infection — can damage the eyes permanently if untreated
- Any conjunctivitis that doesn't improve within a couple of days with veterinary treatment

## What the vet may do
A veterinary eye exam typically includes:
- Checking vision and pupil response
- Examining the front of the eye with magnification and a bright light
- **Fluorescein stain** to look for corneal ulcers — these are common with feline herpesvirus
- Tear production test if dry eye is suspected
- Measuring eye pressure if glaucoma is suspected
- Checking under the lids for foreign material
- Swab or culture for cause-specific testing when needed

Treatment depends on the cause: antiviral and antibiotic drops, oral antiviral for herpesvirus flares, treatment of a respiratory infection, removal of a foreign body, lubrication for dry eye, or treatment of an underlying disease.

## Home care guidance
- **Do not use human eye drops** — redness relievers, antihistamines, and steroid products can mask or worsen serious eye disease in cats.
- Gently wipe discharge away from the outside corner with a clean, moist, lint-free cloth. Use a fresh cloth per eye.
- Prevent the cat from rubbing — an e-collar helps during healing.
- In multi-cat homes, **minimize shared litter boxes, bowls, and bedding** if one cat has an infection, and wash hands between handling.
- Reducing stress can reduce herpesvirus flares — keep routines predictable and consider environmental enrichment.
- **Finish the full prescribed course** of any drops or ointments, even if the eye looks better quickly.
