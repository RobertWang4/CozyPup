## What it is
Diarrhea is the passage of loose or liquid stool, often more frequently than usual. It is a sign of an underlying problem rather than a disease itself. Causes range from diet changes, dietary indiscretion (getting into the trash, eating something unfamiliar), parasites, and stress, to infections (viral, bacterial, protozoal), inflammatory bowel disease, pancreatitis, organ disease, foreign objects, and toxins.

## Acute vs. chronic
- **Acute:** sudden onset, typically lasts less than 2 weeks. Often self-limiting with dietary rest.
- **Chronic:** lasts longer than 2–3 weeks, or keeps recurring. Needs a diagnostic workup.

## What to watch for
- Stool consistency (soft, watery), color (black, red, yellow, greasy), and frequency.
- Presence of blood, mucus, or worms.
- Vomiting, loss of appetite, lethargy, fever, or abdominal pain.
- Dehydration signs: tacky gums, sunken eyes, loss of skin elasticity.
- Recent diet changes, new treats, possible access to garbage, people food, plants, or medications.

## When to seek veterinary care
- Diarrhea lasts more than 24–48 hours, or recurs frequently.
- Blood in the stool, black tarry stool, or profuse watery diarrhea.
- Signs of dehydration, weakness, collapse, or repeated vomiting.
- Diarrhea in a puppy, a senior dog, or a dog with a chronic illness.
- You suspect a toxin, foreign object, or missed vaccines.

## What the vet may do
Diagnostic steps may include a history and physical exam, fecal testing for parasites, bloodwork, imaging (X-ray or ultrasound), and sometimes endoscopy or biopsy for chronic cases. Treatment depends on the cause and can include fluids for hydration, deworming, a temporary diet change, probiotics, or medications targeting the underlying condition.

## Home care guidance
For mild, short-term diarrhea in an otherwise healthy adult dog, the vet may suggest a brief period of dietary rest followed by small, frequent meals of a bland, easily digested food. **Always keep fresh water available** — do not restrict water without veterinary direction, since diarrhea causes fluid loss. Do not give human anti-diarrheal medications without veterinary approval.
