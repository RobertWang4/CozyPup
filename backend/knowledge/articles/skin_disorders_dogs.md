## What it is
"Dermatitis" means any inflammation of the skin. It is a broad label that is often used until a specific cause is identified. Skin disease in dogs can come from external irritants, burns, allergens, trauma, infections (bacterial, fungal, yeast, viral, or parasitic), or from internal conditions that show up on the skin.

## How skin disease shows up
Skin problems can present as any combination of:
- **Itching** (scratching, licking, chewing, rubbing)
- **Redness**, bumps, pimples, scabs, or blisters
- **Hair loss**, thinning, or patchy bald spots
- **Scaling, flaking, or crusting**
- **Thickening or darkening** of the skin over time
- **Odor** or **greasy discharge**
- **Open sores** or wounds from scratching
- Secondary yeast or bacterial infections layered on top of the original problem

A long-standing skin problem often looks different from a fresh one — the early redness can be replaced by thickened, pigmented, dry skin.

## Common causes
- **Fleas and flea allergy** — itching around the tail base, hips, belly.
- **Environmental allergies (atopy)** — often itchy ears, paws, face, armpits; can be seasonal.
- **Food allergies** — year-round itch, ear infections, digestive signs.
- **Mites (mange)** — sarcoptic (very itchy, contagious), demodex (usually not itchy, linked to immune status), ear mites.
- **Bacterial and yeast infections** (secondary to almost any underlying skin issue).
- **Ringworm** (a fungus, contagious to people).
- **Hormonal conditions** (hypothyroidism, Cushing disease) — often symmetrical hair loss, skin changes.

## What to watch for and note for the vet
- When the itching started and how fast it progressed
- Where on the body it is worst (ears, paws, belly, rump)
- Whether other pets or people in the home have skin signs
- Recent travel, boarding, new food, or new products
- Flea and tick prevention status
- Diet, including treats and table scraps
- Any other signs — weight change, drinking and urination changes, behavior changes

## When to see the vet
- Itching that doesn't quickly resolve
- Sores that are spreading, oozing, or smell bad
- Sudden intense scratching or a visible rash
- Hair loss in patches
- Signs of ear infection alongside skin problems
- Anyone in the household is itching or has a rash — some conditions (sarcoptic mange, ringworm) can pass to people

## What the vet may do
A veterinary workup commonly includes a history, a full skin exam, and targeted diagnostics: skin scrapings for mites, tape impressions or cytology for yeast and bacteria, fungal culture for ringworm, and sometimes bloodwork for hormonal disease or allergy testing. Treatment targets both the underlying cause (flea control, diet trial, allergy management, hormone treatment, or anti-parasite medication) and any secondary infections or inflammation.

## Home care guidance
- Keep flea and tick prevention current year-round — many skin problems trace back to fleas.
- Bathe with a shampoo the veterinarian recommends for your dog's specific problem; human shampoos and harsh products often make things worse.
- Prevent additional damage by trimming nails and using an e-collar if the dog is scratching raw spots.
- **Do not apply human topicals, hydrocortisone, or anti-fungal creams** without veterinary approval; some can be toxic if licked.
