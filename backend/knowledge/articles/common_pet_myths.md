## Why this matters
A lot of well-meaning pet advice — from family lore, social media, or outdated websites — turns out to be wrong. Some myths are harmless. Others can delay care, cause injury, or even poison your pet. Below are common North American pet-health myths, with the actual evidence-based picture drawn from Merck, AAHA, AAFP, and the ASPCA.

## Myth 1: "A dry or warm nose means my dog is sick"
**Reality:** Nose moisture and temperature vary throughout the day with activity, sleep, ambient humidity, and what the dog has been doing. A dog waking up from a nap often has a warmer, drier nose; a dog that just drank water has a wet one. Merck's general-care guidance treats nose moisture as an unreliable standalone indicator of illness.

**What is a real warning sign:** lethargy, inappetence, vomiting, diarrhea, breathing changes, gum color changes, fever measured rectally, persistent nasal discharge, or crusting sores on the nose. Those warrant a vet visit — a "dry nose" alone does not.

## Myth 2: "A wagging tail means a happy dog"
**Reality:** Tail wagging is a signal of **arousal**, not specifically happiness. Dogs wag when excited, playful, anxious, conflicted, or aroused before a bite. Context matters: body posture, ear position, mouth tension, eye softness, and the **speed and direction** of the wag all contribute. Research suggests right-biased wags tend to associate with positive states and left-biased wags with negative ones, though this is subtle and not a clinical test.

**Practical takeaway:** never tell a child "he's wagging, he's friendly, go pet him" — read the whole dog, and ask the owner.

## Myth 3: "Dogs and cats eat grass because they're sick"
**Reality:** Grass eating is common in healthy pets and is **poorly understood**. Most dogs that eat grass do not appear ill beforehand and do not vomit afterward. Occasional grazing is generally not a medical concern, though it can be a route for intestinal parasites and pesticide exposure.

**See a vet if:** grass eating is **new, frequent, or consistently followed by vomiting, diarrhea, or loss of appetite** — these can indicate GI disease, nausea, or dietary problems. Keep pets away from treated lawns and toxic plants (see the toxic plants article).

## Myth 4: "One human year equals seven dog years"
**Reality:** The 7:1 rule is a rough cultural shorthand, not biology. Aging is **non-linear**: puppies and kittens mature very quickly in the first two years, and the rate then slows and varies by size and breed. AAHA's canine life-stage guidelines group dogs into **puppy, young adult, mature adult, senior, and end-of-life** stages, with large and giant breeds aging faster than small breeds. A 1-year-old medium dog is roughly a young adult human, not a 7-year-old child.

**Why it matters:** senior screening (bloodwork, blood pressure, dental exams) should start earlier in large breeds — often around age 6–7 — not at the mythical "age 7 = 49".

## Myth 5: "Garlic is good for natural deworming / flea control"
**Reality:** **Garlic is toxic to dogs and cats.** It belongs to the allium family (with onions, leeks, chives) and can damage red blood cells, causing hemolytic anemia. Cats are especially sensitive. The ASPCA lists garlic on its People Foods to Avoid list, and Merck lists allium toxicity in its food-hazards chapter.

**Use evidence-based parasite prevention** from a veterinarian — safe, effective oral or topical products are widely available. Do not use garlic, essential oils, or folk remedies as dewormers or flea treatments.

## Myth 6: "Cats always land on their feet"
**Reality:** Cats have a remarkable righting reflex, but they do **not** always land safely. "High-rise syndrome" is a well-documented veterinary emergency — cats falling from upper-floor windows or balconies sustain broken jaws, chest trauma, limb fractures, and internal injuries. Merck's trauma chapters and emergency literature describe it clearly. Paradoxically, falls from moderate heights (2–6 stories) can be as injurious as higher falls because the cat has less time to rotate and relax.

**Prevention:** secure screens on all open windows and balconies, especially in high-rise apartments. If your cat falls any distance, **take them to a vet immediately**, even if they seem fine — internal bleeding and chest injuries may not be obvious at first.

## Myth 7: "Spaying or neutering causes obesity"
**Reality:** Spay/neuter surgery itself does not cause weight gain. What actually happens is that **metabolic rate decreases** modestly after surgery, and many pets are neutered right around the time they transition from puppy/kitten growth to adult maintenance, when their calorie needs drop anyway. If feeding continues at the old amount, weight gain follows — but the **root cause is overfeeding**, not the surgery.

**What to do:** at the post-op recheck, ask your vet to reassess body condition and adjust daily calories (often by 20–30%). Measure portions. Weigh the pet monthly. The benefits of spay/neuter (reduced mammary cancer, pyometra, testicular cancer, roaming, and some behavior problems) far outweigh the manageable calorie adjustment.

## Myth 8: "Indoor cats don't need vaccinations"
**Reality:** The AAFP Feline Vaccination Advisory Panel recommends **core vaccines for all cats**, including indoor-only cats:
- **FVRCP** (feline viral rhinotracheitis / calicivirus / panleukopenia) — panleukopenia virus is extremely hardy and can be tracked in on shoes and clothing.
- **Rabies** — required by law in most US states and Canadian provinces, and essential if the cat ever bites a person or is exposed to wildlife (bats can enter homes).

Non-core vaccines (like FeLV) depend on lifestyle and exposure risk. Indoor cats can still escape, be boarded, visit the vet, or be exposed to rabid bats. Vaccination is not optional just because the door stays closed.

## When to trust your gut and call the vet
Myths exist because the real signs of illness are often subtle. Trust your instincts and contact a veterinarian if your pet shows:
- A change in appetite, drinking, urination, or activity lasting more than 24–48 hours
- Vomiting or diarrhea that is repeated, bloody, or accompanied by lethargy
- Breathing changes, coughing, collapse, or pale/blue gums (**emergency**)
- Any trauma, toxin exposure, or fall — **don't wait for symptoms**
- Behavioral shifts that seem out of character

When information on the internet conflicts with your vet's advice, your vet — who can actually examine your pet — wins. When in doubt, call.
