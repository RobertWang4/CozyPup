## What it is
Vaccinations help a dog's immune system recognize and respond to specific infectious diseases. Core vaccines are the ones recommended for essentially all dogs because the diseases they prevent are widespread, severe, or transmissible to people. Non-core vaccines are added based on geography and lifestyle.

## Core vaccines
Core vaccines for dogs typically cover:
- **Distemper**
- **Parvovirus**
- **Adenovirus (infectious hepatitis)**
- **Rabies** (required by law in the US and Canada)

## Non-core vaccines (lifestyle-based)
Depending on where you live and what your dog does, the veterinarian may also recommend vaccines for:
- **Leptospirosis**
- **Bordetella** (kennel cough) — boarding, daycare, grooming, dog parks
- **Lyme disease** — tick-exposure regions
- **Canine influenza**

The choice depends on regional risk and the dog's activities. Your veterinarian is the best source for the exact schedule.

## Puppies
Puppies need a vaccine series, usually given every 3 to 4 weeks until about 16 weeks old. Immunity from the mother's antibodies gradually fades during this window, so boosters are timed to pick up protection as the pup becomes susceptible. Rabies is typically given once the puppy is old enough under local law.

## Adult dogs
Adult dogs receive booster shots on a schedule set by their veterinarian. Some vaccines only need boosting every few years; others are given annually. Your vet will track the schedule.

## When to ask the vet about timing
- You just adopted a puppy or an adult dog with unknown vaccination history.
- You are moving, traveling, boarding, or starting daycare.
- Your dog is due or overdue for a booster.
- Your dog had a reaction to a previous vaccine (report this before the next dose).
- Your dog is pregnant, very young, very old, or being treated for an illness.

## What to watch for after a vaccine
Mild soreness, reduced appetite, or a quiet day are common and usually pass within 24–48 hours. Contact the veterinarian promptly if you see facial swelling, hives, persistent vomiting, difficulty breathing, or collapse — these can signal an allergic reaction and need immediate care.
