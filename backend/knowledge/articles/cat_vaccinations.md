## What it is
Vaccinations help a cat's immune system recognize and fight off specific infectious diseases. They are important for **all cats — both indoor-only and outdoor-going** — because many of the diseases are easy to encounter even in a seemingly safe home environment.

## Core vaccines
Core vaccines are the ones recommended for essentially all cats. They typically protect against:
- **Feline panleukopenia (feline distemper)**
- **Feline herpesvirus (feline viral rhinotracheitis)**
- **Feline calicivirus**
- **Rabies** (required by law in most of the US and Canada)

The first three are often combined into the "FVRCP" vaccine.

## Non-core vaccines (lifestyle-based)
Depending on age, lifestyle, and risk, the vet may also recommend:
- **Feline leukemia virus (FeLV)** — especially for kittens, outdoor cats, and multi-cat homes
- Others per local guidelines and outbreak risk

The veterinarian chooses the right set based on whether the cat goes outside, shares a home with other cats, travels, boards, or has known medical conditions.

## Kittens
Kittens need a vaccine series, typically every 3 to 4 weeks until they are about 6 months old. Maternal antibodies fade during this window, so multiple boosters ensure protection as the kitten becomes susceptible. Rabies is given according to age and local law.

## Adult cats
Adult cats receive booster shots throughout life. Some boosters are needed every few years; others may be annual. The veterinarian tracks the schedule.

## When to ask the vet
- You just adopted a kitten or a cat with unknown vaccination history
- Your cat is due or overdue for a booster
- Your cat had a reaction to a previous vaccine
- Your cat is pregnant, elderly, or being treated for another illness
- Your cat's lifestyle is changing (going outdoors, moving, boarding)

## What to watch for after a vaccine
Mild soreness at the injection site, a quiet day, or a low-grade fever are common and usually pass within 24–48 hours. **Contact the veterinarian promptly** if you see facial swelling, hives, persistent vomiting, difficulty breathing, collapse, or a firm lump at the injection site that lasts more than a few weeks — the vet will want to examine that lump.
