## When is a cat "senior"?
Rough framework in common veterinary use:
- **Mature**: about **7–10 years**
- **Senior**: about **10–14 years**
- **Geriatric**: **14+ years**

Cats are very good at hiding illness. Many diseases that shorten feline lives — kidney disease, hyperthyroidism, dental pain, arthritis, diabetes, heart disease, some cancers — are **detectable before symptoms appear** with routine bloodwork.

## What changes with age
- **Metabolism slows**; some cats gain, many quietly lose weight as muscle wastes
- **Chronic kidney disease** is common — early signs are increased drinking and urination
- **Hyperthyroidism** is common — weight loss despite good appetite, hyperactivity, vocalization
- **Dental disease** and **tooth resorption** cause silent pain
- **Arthritis** is very common and very underdiagnosed in cats — cats don't limp like dogs, they just stop jumping or become grumpy when touched
- **Sensory decline** — vision and hearing gradually reduce
- **Cognitive changes** ("feline cognitive dysfunction") — night vocalization, disorientation, changes in litter box use
- **Reduced grooming** — a matted or greasy coat can be a sign of pain, arthritis, or illness
- **Lumps, skin changes, personality changes**

## The senior cat care checklist

### 1. See the vet twice a year
Senior and geriatric cats should be examined **every 6 months**. Expect the vet to recommend:
- Physical exam, body condition scoring, muscle scoring, dental, lumps
- **Weight tracked every visit** — unintended weight loss is a key early signal
- **Bloodwork** (CBC, chemistry, **T4 thyroid**) — often twice yearly in geriatric cats
- **Urinalysis** — early kidney disease shows up in urine first
- **Blood pressure** — high blood pressure is common in older cats and can cause sudden blindness
- Parasite prevention and vaccines per lifestyle

### 2. Signs worth noting and reporting
- **Drinking more or peeing more** — kidney disease, diabetes, hyperthyroidism
- **Weight change** in either direction
- **Increased appetite with weight loss** — classic for hyperthyroidism
- **Decreased appetite or pickiness** — dental pain, kidney disease, nausea, many causes
- **Vomiting** more than occasionally — not normal, even in cats
- **Not jumping up**, hesitating on stairs, missing on jumps — often arthritis
- **Matted, greasy, or unkempt coat**, dandruff — arthritis, pain, or systemic illness
- **Night vocalization**, restlessness, disorientation — cognitive dysfunction or hypertension
- **Urinating or defecating outside the box** — medical causes first; arthritis can make box entry painful
- **Lumps, breath changes, eye or ear discharge**
- **Sudden blindness or stumbling** — could be hypertension, emergency

### 3. Mobility and home environment
- **Lower-sided litter boxes** in multiple spots (arthritic cats hate stepping high).
- **Soft, non-slip bedding** at warm, draft-free spots.
- **Step stools or ramps** to favorite perches.
- **Shorter jumps** — move food to the floor if countertop eating is hard.
- **Claws trimmed** regularly — older cats may not wear them down and they can grow into the paw pad.
- Keep the environment predictable; avoid unnecessary furniture rearrangement.

### 4. Nutrition and water
- Many senior cats benefit from **increased water intake** — wet food, fountains, multiple water stations — to support kidneys and bladder.
- Diet choice depends on bloodwork and diagnoses (renal, urinary, weight management). **Don't choose "senior" food by name alone** — ask the vet what fits this cat.
- **Weigh monthly at home** — a pound lost is a lot on a 9-pound cat.
- Feed on a schedule; track what's actually eaten (not just what's offered).

### 5. Dental care
Dental disease is extremely common in older cats and extremely painful. Home brushing helps; regular professional cleanings with full-mouth X-rays are often necessary. Cats can have **tooth resorption** that is not visible without X-rays.

### 6. Pain management
Cats rarely "tell" you they hurt. Reduced grooming, withdrawal, grumpiness when touched, and reduced jumping are pain signs. Safe long-term pain management for cats is possible — **with a veterinarian**. Never give human painkillers; acetaminophen (Tylenol) is deadly to cats.

### 7. Cognitive health
For cats showing night vocalization, confusion, or altered litter box use, rule out medical causes first (hypertension, hyperthyroidism, dental pain, kidney disease) before assuming dementia. Environmental enrichment, predictable routines, nightlights, and — in some cases — prescribed medication can help.

## When to go to the vet sooner
- Sudden collapse, weakness, or trouble breathing
- Straining in the litter box with little or no urine (possible urinary blockage — **emergency**, especially in males)
- Seizure
- Sudden blindness or stumbling
- Sudden onset of yowling in obvious pain
- Not eating for more than 24–36 hours — cats can develop **hepatic lipidosis** (liver failure) from just a few days of not eating
- Blood in vomit, stool, or urine
- Yellow gums or eye whites (jaundice)

## A realistic note
Quality of life — appetite, grooming, interest in surroundings, comfort, predictable litter box — is the goal. Regular check-ins with your vet about **what a "good day" looks like** for this cat help guide decisions at every stage.
