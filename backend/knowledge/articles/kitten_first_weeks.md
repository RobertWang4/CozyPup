## When to bring a kitten home
Most kittens are ready to leave mom and littermates at about **8–12 weeks old**. By then they are eating on their own, using a litter box, and have had early socialization.

## First-week checklist

### Vet visit
- Schedule the first vet appointment within the **first 1–2 weeks** of bringing the kitten home.
- The vet will do a physical exam, start or continue the vaccine series, check a stool sample, start parasite prevention, and discuss diet, behavior, and socialization.
- **Bring any paperwork** from the breeder, shelter, or previous vet.
- **Discuss testing for FeLV and FIV**, especially if the kitten's history is unknown.

### Vaccinations
Kittens receive some immunity from their mother, which fades during the first few months. Core vaccines (feline panleukopenia, feline herpesvirus, feline calicivirus — often given as "FVRCP" — plus rabies) are given in a series, usually every 3–4 weeks until the kitten is about 6 months old. **FeLV vaccination** is commonly recommended for kittens because of their higher susceptibility, regardless of whether they will go outside.

### Parasite control
Kittens commonly have intestinal worms from their mother, and they can pick up more from fleas or the environment. The vet will set a deworming schedule and usually start a year-round parasite preventive (intestinal worms, fleas, often heartworm). Repeat fecal testing is done until clear.

### Nutrition
- Feed a **complete and balanced kitten food** labeled by AAFCO as appropriate for growth or all life stages.
- Kittens grow fast and need frequent meals — typically three to four times a day, reducing to twice daily as they approach adulthood (around 10–12 months).
- **Fresh water always available.** Cats often under-drink; a mix of wet and dry food and multiple water sources helps.
- Avoid cow's milk — many cats are lactose intolerant.
- **Never give human food that is toxic to cats** (onion, garlic, chocolate, grapes/raisins, xylitol, alcohol).

### Home setup
- **Litter box:** one per cat, plus one extra; place in a quiet, accessible spot away from food and water; use an unscented clumping litter to start and note preferences. Scoop daily.
- **Feeding and water stations:** separate from the litter box. Multiple small stations are better than one busy spot.
- **Scratching posts:** multiple, tall (so the cat can fully stretch), of different textures (sisal, cardboard, carpet). Offer these before furniture becomes the default.
- **Vertical space:** cat trees, shelves, perches — cats feel safer with high vantage points.
- **Hiding spots:** boxes, covered beds, or accessible closets, especially in the first few days.
- **Safety:** secure toxic plants (especially **no lilies — lilies can kill cats**), cleaning products, medications, strings/ribbons/hair ties/dental floss (all are choking and intestinal-obstruction risks), rubber bands, open windows, dryers, reclining chairs.

## Socialization and handling
The early months are the key window for confident behavior. Expose the kitten positively to:
- Different people (gentle, quiet introductions)
- Different sounds at a comfortable volume
- **Carrier time** — leave it out as a comfy space with treats, not just a "vet trip" object
- **Handling exercises** — paws, mouth, ears, brushing — brief and paired with treats
- Short car rides to a positive place (friend's house) so the car isn't only associated with the vet
- Safe play with toys (**never hands or feet** — it encourages biting)

## When to call the vet right away
- Repeated vomiting or diarrhea, especially with blood
- Lethargy, not eating, hiding beyond the first day or two in a new home
- Pale, yellow, or blue gums
- Coughing, sneezing with yellow/green discharge, eye discharge, difficulty breathing
- **Straining to urinate with little or no urine** — emergency, especially in male kittens
- Seizures, tremors, or sudden collapse
- Swollen, painful, or distended belly
- Suspected toxin exposure (see our toxic-foods and plants articles)
- Signs of trauma, bite wounds, or severe limp
- **Possible lily exposure — any amount, any part**, for a cat is an emergency

Kittens can decline fast. **Call sooner rather than later** with any concern.

## Spay/neuter
Most kittens are spayed or neutered at about 5–6 months of age, though shelter and veterinary practice may schedule it earlier. The timing should be discussed with your veterinarian based on the kitten's size, breed, and lifestyle.
