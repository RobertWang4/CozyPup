## Heat cycles in cats
Female cats ("queens") are **seasonal reflex ovulators** — they come into heat repeatedly during the longer daylight months and ovulate only when mated. Heats typically last **4–7 days** and may come back every **2–3 weeks** if the cat is not mated.

Typical signs of a cat in heat:
- Very loud, insistent yowling (easily mistaken for pain)
- Restlessness, rubbing on everything, rolling
- Raising the rear with the tail to one side when touched on the lower back
- Escaping attempts
- Usually **no visible bleeding** (unlike dogs)

First heat can occur as early as **4–5 months old**, earlier in some breeds. **Unspayed indoor cats in heat often escape** looking for mates, so the simplest prevention of unwanted pregnancy is to **spay before the first heat** unless breeding responsibly.

## Pregnancy basics
- **Gestation length**: about **63–65 days** from ovulation.
- **Confirming pregnancy**: palpation by the vet around 3–4 weeks, ultrasound from day 21–28, X-ray from about day 40–45 (to count kittens and plan for delivery).
- **Nutrition**: switch to a **kitten or all-life-stages food** in the second half of pregnancy and throughout lactation — a nursing queen needs 2–3× her usual calories.
- **Medications and vaccines**: do not start new medications, flea products, or vaccines without veterinary approval — several are unsafe in pregnancy, and some flea products are dangerous if they reach nursing kittens.
- **Environment**: quiet, stable home; avoid stressful changes in the last weeks.

## Getting ready for queening
- Provide a **clean, warm, quiet queening box** in a low-traffic room at least a week before the due date; queens often choose their own spot (closet, under the bed).
- Keep the **vet's after-hours number** handy.
- Have on hand: clean towels, unwaxed dental floss (to tie cords if needed), a digital thermometer, a scale, a notebook.
- Keep other pets and small children away once labor starts.

## Stages of labor
**Stage 1 (preparation)**: restlessness, nesting, panting, decreased appetite. Typically a few hours to a day. Some queens become unusually affectionate; others hide.
**Stage 2 (active)**: visible contractions and kittens delivered, usually one every **10–60 minutes** for most queens. Total litter delivery may span several hours.
**Stage 3 (placenta)**: a placenta follows each kitten; the queen usually chews the cord and licks the kitten clean.

**Normal interrupted labor in cats**: some queens deliver one or two kittens, then rest for **up to 24 hours** before resuming — this is unique to cats and not automatically a problem if the queen is calm, comfortable, and caring for the kittens she's delivered. **But** if she is straining, distressed, or bleeding, it's not normal.

## Emergencies during queening — call or go to the vet immediately
- **Strong contractions for more than 30 minutes without producing a kitten**
- Labor not started **past day 68** of pregnancy
- A **visible kitten stuck** that doesn't progress within a few minutes
- A **foul-smelling, green, or bloody** discharge without a kitten following promptly (placenta has separated — emergency)
- Queen is **exhausted, trembling, weak, collapsed, or unresponsive**
- **Heavy ongoing bleeding**
- Mother **ignoring or harming** kittens — she may be in pain or post-delivery complications
- **Shivering, twitching, agitation, seizures** after delivery — possible **eclampsia** (low blood calcium), an emergency in a nursing queen

## Normal postpartum and warning signs

### Normal
- Reddish-brown discharge (lochia) for 1–3 weeks, gradually decreasing
- Greatly increased appetite
- Protective and attentive behavior
- Gradual weight loss as kittens grow

### Call the vet
- **Foul-smelling or heavy bloody discharge**
- **Fever** (above 103°F / 39.4°C)
- **No appetite** or obvious sickness
- **Swollen, hot, painful mammary gland** — mastitis
- **Not producing milk**, or kittens not gaining weight or constantly crying
- **Twitching, restlessness, wobbling, seizures in mother** — possible **eclampsia**
- Number of placentas did not match number of kittens

## Caring for newborn kittens
- Keep the queening area **warm — 85–90°F (29–32°C)** the first week, then gradually lower.
- Kittens nurse every 2–4 hours initially. **Weigh daily** — steady gain is the best early-warning sign.
- Umbilical cords usually dry and fall off within a week.
- Vet visit at 2–3 weeks for first exam + deworming plan.
- Milestones: eyes open around 7–14 days, walking around 3 weeks, weaning begins around 4 weeks. First vaccines usually at 8–9 weeks.
