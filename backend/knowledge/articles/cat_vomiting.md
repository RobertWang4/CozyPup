## What it is
Vomiting is the forceful ejection of stomach or upper small-intestine contents, usually preceded by drooling, repeated swallowing, retching, and abdominal contractions. In cats, vomiting can be caused by digestive disease, kidney or liver problems, pancreatitis, parasites, foreign objects, food allergies, toxin exposure, infections, certain medications, tumors, and, in some cases, **hairballs**.

Vomiting is different from **regurgitation**, which is passive — the food is undigested and often tube-shaped and is not accompanied by abdominal effort.

## Hairballs
Cats swallow loose hair when grooming. Hair cannot be digested, so it tends to ball up in the stomach and is either passed through the digestive tract or vomited up. Occasional hairballs are common, especially in long-haired breeds. **But frequent hairballs are not normal.** Hair that stays in the stomach can harden and even block the digestive tract. Regular brushing and cat-specific hairball diets can help. Over-the-counter hairball remedies should only be used on veterinary advice. If your cat brings up hairballs often — or is trying to and nothing comes up — see the veterinarian; another problem may be the real cause.

## Acute vs. chronic
- **Short-term or occasional:** a cat that has vomited once or twice in 1–2 days and seems otherwise well.
- **Long-term or severe:** vomiting more than once or twice a day, or vomiting that comes with blood, belly pain, lethargy, dehydration, weakness, fever, or weight loss. Some causes are life-threatening.

## What to note for the veterinarian
- How often and when (before/after meals, mornings on an empty stomach)
- What the vomit looks like (food, fluid, foam, bile, blood, hair)
- Other signs: diarrhea, appetite loss, hiding, weight change, drinking more or less
- Access to houseplants, strings, ribbons, hair ties, medications, or other household items
- Recent diet change; outdoor access; flea/tick status

## When to seek veterinary care
- Vomiting continues more than 24 hours, or happens repeatedly in a day
- Blood in the vomit, or material that looks like coffee grounds
- The cat is lethargic, hiding, not eating, weak, or visibly dehydrated
- A possible foreign object swallowed — **especially string, yarn, ribbon, or dental floss**
- Abdominal pain, swelling, or distress
- A kitten, a senior cat, or a cat with kidney, liver, thyroid, or heart disease

## What the vet may do
For short-term mild vomiting, supportive care may be all that's needed. For chronic or severe cases, evaluation commonly includes history, physical exam, bloodwork, urinalysis, fecal testing, X-rays, and ultrasound. Endoscopy with biopsy is sometimes needed to reach a diagnosis. Hospitalization with IV fluids is used for dehydrated or severely ill cats.

## Home care guidance
Follow veterinary instructions for the specific case. A common approach for mild, short-term vomiting in an otherwise healthy adult cat is a brief period of dietary rest followed by small, bland meals — but **never withhold water unless the vet has put the cat on fluids**, since cats dehydrate quickly and cats with any kidney or heart problem are especially vulnerable. Do not give human anti-nausea medications.
