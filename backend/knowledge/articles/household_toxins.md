## Why this matters
Many everyday household products are highly poisonous to dogs and cats. Some, like **antifreeze (ethylene glycol)**, are deadly in very small amounts and have a treatment window measured in hours. Others, like rodenticides, cause internal bleeding or neurological damage days after exposure. If you see or suspect your pet has contacted any of the substances below, **treat it as an emergency and call a poison control hotline immediately** — do not wait for symptoms.

**US/Canada 24/7 helplines:**
- ASPCA Animal Poison Control Center: **(888) 426-4435**
- Pet Poison Helpline: **(855) 764-7661**

## Antifreeze (ethylene glycol) — the headline hazard
Ethylene glycol is the main ingredient in most automotive antifreeze and coolant, some windshield de-icers, and certain brake fluids. It tastes sweet, which attracts dogs and cats, and **as little as a few teaspoons can be fatal to a dog, and even less to a cat**. The treatment window is very tight — antidote therapy is most effective when started within a few hours of ingestion.

Signs come in waves and can be mistaken for other problems:
- **Early (30 min – 12 hours):** wobbly or "drunk" gait, stumbling, vomiting, increased thirst and urination, lethargy.
- **Apparent recovery (12–24 hours):** the pet may look better — this is deceptive.
- **Late (1–3 days):** severe kidney failure — vomiting, not urinating, mouth ulcers, collapse, often fatal.

**What to do:** call a poison helpline or emergency vet the instant you suspect exposure, even if the pet looks fine. Bring the product container. Every hour matters.

## Cleaning products — bleach, ammonia, toilet cleaners, detergents
Household cleaners range from mildly irritating to severely corrosive. The worst are concentrated drain cleaners, oven cleaners, toilet bowl cleaners, and lye-based products; bleach and ammonia are usually irritant rather than strongly caustic but can still cause serious injury, especially if mixed (bleach + ammonia releases toxic chloramine gas).

Signs may include drooling, pawing at the mouth, vomiting (sometimes bloody), burns on the tongue or lips, difficulty swallowing, and coughing or labored breathing from fumes.

**Important:** for corrosive or caustic products, **do not induce vomiting** — re-exposure burns the esophagus a second time. Do not give water or milk unless a poison-control professional tells you to. Go to the vet with the product label.

## Rodenticides (rat and mouse poisons)
Rodenticides are a common accidental poisoning and there are several different classes — each with different signs and antidotes. **Always bring the packaging** so the vet can identify the active ingredient.

- **Anticoagulant rodenticides** (brodifacoum, bromadiolone, warfarin): block vitamin K and cause internal bleeding. Signs often appear 3–5 days after ingestion: weakness, pale gums, coughing blood, nosebleeds, bruising, bloody urine or stool.
- **Bromethalin**: causes brain swelling. Signs include tremors, incoordination, hind-leg weakness, seizures, paralysis. No specific antidote.
- **Cholecalciferol (vitamin D3)**: causes dangerously high calcium and kidney failure. Early signs are vague — increased thirst, vomiting, weakness — kidney damage follows.
- **Zinc/aluminum phosphide** (mole and gopher baits): releases toxic phosphine gas in the stomach; vomit can be dangerous to humans nearby.

Any suspected rodenticide exposure is an emergency, even if your pet seems fine.

## Insecticides and pyrethrins/pyrethroids
Flea and tick products labeled for **dogs** that contain concentrated pyrethroids (permethrin) are highly toxic to cats and can cause tremors, seizures, drooling, and death. Never use a dog flea product on a cat. Organophosphates and carbamates (older yard and pest products) are also serious poisonings.

## Essential oils
Concentrated essential oils — tea tree (melaleuca), pennyroyal, wintergreen, pine, citrus, eucalyptus, cinnamon, peppermint, ylang-ylang — can cause drooling, vomiting, weakness, tremors, and liver damage. **Cats are especially sensitive** because they lack a liver enzyme needed to metabolize these compounds; skin contact, ingestion, and even diffusion into the air can affect them.

## What to do if exposure is suspected
1. **Move the pet away** from the substance and to a safe area. Do not leave the product within reach of other pets or children.
2. **Find the packaging or a photo of the label.** Active ingredient, concentration, and amount consumed all change treatment.
3. **Call a poison helpline or your vet immediately** — do not wait to see if symptoms develop. Many poisons cause damage that is much easier to prevent than to reverse.
4. **Do not induce vomiting unless poison control tells you to.** For corrosives, petroleum products, sharp objects, and for pets that are already weak, seizing, or unconscious, vomiting causes further harm.
5. **Do not give home remedies** (milk, salt, hydrogen peroxide, activated charcoal) unless specifically directed — timing and dose matter and can make things worse.
6. **Transport the pet** with the packaging. Call ahead so the clinic is ready.

## Emergency numbers
- **ASPCA Animal Poison Control Center (24/7):** (888) 426-4435
- **Pet Poison Helpline (24/7):** (855) 764-7661

Both charge a consultation fee, but the call can save your pet's life and is usually faster and more specific than a general vet line.

## Prevention
- Store antifreeze, cleaners, pesticides, and rodent baits in closed cabinets, ideally latched. Clean up antifreeze spills immediately — a puddle in the garage is enough to kill.
- Consider **propylene glycol-based** ("pet-safer") antifreeze; it is still not harmless but is far less toxic than ethylene glycol.
- Never apply a dog flea product to a cat. Read labels.
- Keep essential oil diffusers out of rooms cats use; store oils out of reach.
- After using bait stations or sprays, keep pets out of the treated area for the label-specified time.
