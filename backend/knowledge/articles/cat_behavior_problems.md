## What it is
Behavior problems in cats include any pattern that is distressing, unsafe, or incompatible with the household. The most common are **urine and feces outside the litter box, scratching furniture, aggression (toward people or other cats), excessive vocalization, and signs of anxiety or fear**. Many "behavior" problems are actually driven by **pain, illness, or environmental stress** — and effective treatment usually means fixing the underlying cause, not just correcting the cat.

**First step: rule out a medical cause.** A cat straining outside the litter box can be in a urinary emergency; sudden aggression can come from pain; a vocal senior cat can have hyperthyroidism or cognitive decline. A veterinary exam comes first.

## Common presentations

### House-soiling (peeing or pooping outside the box)
This is the single most common feline behavior complaint. Causes include:
- **Medical:** FLUTD (feline lower urinary tract disease), urinary infection, bladder stones, constipation, kidney disease, diabetes, hyperthyroidism, arthritis (hard to step into a high-sided box).
- **Litter-box issues:** too few boxes (rule of thumb: one per cat + one extra), unclean boxes, disliked litter, covered or scented boxes, box in a loud/busy spot, boxes near food.
- **Marking (usually vertical spraying against walls):** more common in intact males, multi-cat households, and during territorial stress.
- **Stress:** new pet, new baby, moving, new furniture, neighborhood cats visible through windows.

**A cat straining to urinate and producing little or nothing — especially a male cat — is an emergency. Go to the vet immediately.**

### Scratching furniture
Scratching is normal, necessary behavior — it maintains claws, marks territory, and stretches the body. The goal is to **redirect** scratching to acceptable surfaces, not eliminate it. Offer multiple scratching posts of different types (sisal, cardboard, carpet), in visible locations where the cat already likes to stretch (near sleeping spots, near doorways). Make unwanted surfaces less appealing (double-sided tape, covers) while the cat learns the new spots.

**Declawing** (amputation of the toe bones) is not recommended by most veterinary organizations and is illegal or restricted in many jurisdictions; it does not fix underlying behavior and often creates new problems.

### Aggression
- **Petting-induced** — cats that enjoy stroking briefly then turn and bite/swat; watch body language (tail flicking, flattened ears, rippling skin) and stop before the threshold.
- **Play aggression** — kittens and young cats that stalk, pounce, and bite hands or ankles. Redirect onto toys; never use hands as play objects.
- **Fear aggression** — directed at strangers, vet visits, or other pets.
- **Redirected aggression** — cat sees/hears a trigger (outdoor cat through the window), cannot reach it, and attacks whoever is nearest.
- **Inter-cat aggression** — between household cats; often linked to resource competition (food, litter, resting spots) or incomplete introductions.
- **Pain-related** — sudden aggression in a previously tolerant cat deserves a vet exam.

### Excessive vocalization
Persistent yowling, especially at night, has many causes including hyperthyroidism, hypertension, pain, cognitive dysfunction (in older cats), hunger, attention-seeking, and heat cycles (in intact females). In older cats, start with a vet workup.

### Anxiety, fear, and compulsive behaviors
Hiding, over-grooming to the point of bald patches, excessive self-soothing behaviors, and pacing can signal anxiety. Over-grooming is often linked to stress or a medical itch — both should be investigated.

## What works (evidence-based approach)
- **Rule out medical causes first.**
- **Environmental enrichment** matters a lot for cats: vertical space (cat trees, shelves), window perches, food puzzles, short daily play sessions, hiding places. Many feline behavior problems respond to a richer environment.
- **Litter-box strategy**: enough boxes, scoop daily, full refresh every 1–2 weeks, unscented clumping litter in an open low-sided box in a quiet spot for most cats.
- **Gradual introductions** between cats (smell, sight, supervised contact over days to weeks), not "throw them together."
- **Positive reinforcement and redirection** — reward calm behavior, redirect unwanted behavior, never use physical punishment (it increases fear and often backfires into aggression or house-soiling).
- **Pheromone products** (e.g., Feliway) may help some cats, especially with marking and inter-cat conflict.
- **Medication** under a veterinarian's care when anxiety or aggression is severe.
- **Professional help** — a veterinary behaviorist (DACVB) or a certified cat behavior consultant for serious or unsafe cases.

## What to avoid
- Punishment (spraying with water, yelling, "scruffing," rubbing nose in urine) — damages the cat's trust and usually worsens the behavior.
- Declawing as a behavior solution.
- Ignoring sudden changes — in cats, new behavior almost always deserves a medical check first.

## When to see the veterinarian or a behaviorist
- **Any straining in the litter box, especially in a male cat** — emergency.
- Sudden change in behavior in an adult cat.
- Aggression that has resulted in biting or puncture wounds.
- Over-grooming causing bald spots or sores.
- Excessive vocalization, disorientation, or altered sleep in a senior cat.
- Anxiety or aggression that prevents the cat from eating, using the box, or being handled for basic care.
