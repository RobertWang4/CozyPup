## What it is
Ear mites (*Otodectes cynotis*) are tiny parasites that live in the external ear canal, though they can sometimes be found on the body as well. They are more common in cats than in dogs, but dogs — especially puppies and dogs from shelters or multi-pet homes — can also be infested. Ear mites are contagious between animals in close contact.

## Signs to watch for
- **Head shaking** and scratching at the ears
- **Ears held low or drooping** in a dog that normally carries them upright
- **Dark, crumbly, coffee-ground-like debris** in the ear canal
- **Ear odor** and visible redness or swelling of the ear canal
- **Scratching around the head and neck** severe enough to cause sores
- In severe cases, pus, bleeding, or a torn eardrum

The itching can be intense. Secondary bacterial or yeast infections often develop on top of the mite infestation.

## When to see the vet
- Any new or worsening ear scratching, head shaking, or ear discharge
- You see dark debris deep in the ear canal
- Your dog is in pain when you touch the ear, or the ear flap is swollen
- You have more than one pet in the home and one is showing ear signs — others should also be checked

Ear mites look similar to bacterial and yeast ear infections from the outside, but the treatment is different, so a veterinary diagnosis matters.

## What the vet may do
The veterinarian typically looks in the ear with an otoscope, takes a sample of the debris, and examines it under a microscope to identify mites. The vet will also check for secondary bacterial or yeast infection and for damage to the eardrum, which changes which medications are safe.

Treatment usually involves:
- **Cleaning the ear canal** to remove debris.
- **A parasiticide** applied in the ears, topically on the body (spot-on), or given orally — the choice depends on the dog.
- **Treating secondary infections** if present.
- **Treating all other pets in the home** that have been in contact, since mites pass easily between animals.

Follow-up appointments confirm the mites are gone.

## Home care guidance
- Complete the **full course of treatment** even if the dog looks better early — mites have a life cycle, and stopping early allows them to come back.
- Wash bedding, toys, and grooming tools; vacuum common resting spots.
- **Do not use home remedies, human ear drops, or generic "ear cleaners" without veterinary guidance** — some are painful, can damage the eardrum, or are toxic.
- Keep flea, tick, and parasite prevention up to date; some monthly products also help cover ear mites.
