## What it is
**Feline lower urinary tract disease (FLUTD)** is an umbrella term for a group of conditions affecting the bladder and urethra in cats. It is common, often recurrent, and, in its most severe form — **urethral obstruction** — life-threatening and a true emergency.

Causes and contributors include:
- **Feline idiopathic cystitis (FIC)** — bladder inflammation with no identifiable infection, often stress-linked; the most common cause in younger cats.
- **Bladder stones or crystals**.
- **Urethral plugs** (most common in male cats).
- **Bacterial urinary tract infections** — more common in older cats, cats with kidney disease, or cats with diabetes.
- **Bladder tumors** — uncommon, usually in older cats.

## Why male cats are at special risk
The male cat's urethra is long and narrow and can become **completely blocked** by crystals, mucus, or a stone. A blocked cat cannot urinate. Within 24–48 hours this can cause severe electrolyte imbalance, kidney injury, and death. **This is an emergency.**

## Signs to watch for
- **Straining to urinate** — squatting repeatedly, sometimes in odd places
- **Frequent trips to the litter box** with little or no urine
- **Blood in the urine** (pink, red, or brown urine)
- **Crying, howling, or hiding** when urinating; obvious signs of pain
- **Urinating outside the litter box** — this is often a medical sign, not a behavior problem
- **Over-grooming the belly or genital area**
- **Vomiting, lethargy, weakness, or collapse** — in a blocked cat, these indicate a critical emergency

## When to seek veterinary care
- **Emergency — go immediately** if your cat is straining with no urine produced, is crying, is vomiting, is weak or collapsed, or has a hard, painful belly. A suspected urethral obstruction cannot wait.
- Promptly, if your cat is straining, going frequently, has bloody urine, or is urinating in odd places.
- If signs keep coming back — FLUTD often recurs and needs a long-term management plan.

## What the vet may do
Workup usually includes physical exam, urinalysis, urine culture, bloodwork, and imaging (X-rays and/or ultrasound) to check for stones, crystals, or a thickened bladder wall.

Treatment depends on the cause. A blocked cat needs immediate sedation, urinary catheterization to relieve the obstruction, IV fluids, pain control, and hospitalization. Cats with idiopathic cystitis are managed with pain relief, stress reduction, and changes at home. Stones and some crystal types may be managed with prescription diets; others require surgery. Infections are treated with antibiotics chosen from culture.

## Home care (for prevention and between episodes)
Even outside a crisis, home environment matters a lot in FLUTD:
- **Increase water intake** — multiple fresh water sources, wider bowls (cats often dislike whisker contact with narrow bowls), pet water fountains, and canned/wet food. More water means more dilute urine.
- **Enough litter boxes** — the general guideline is one per cat plus one extra; keep them clean and in quiet spots.
- **Reduce stress** — conflict with other cats, changes in routine, and crowding can trigger idiopathic cystitis. Vertical space, hiding spots, predictable feeding times, and enrichment all help.
- **Weight control** — overweight cats have higher FLUTD risk.
- **Follow the prescribed diet** if one is recommended; do not add unprescribed supplements.
- **Track symptoms** and bring the list to every vet visit so patterns can be addressed.
