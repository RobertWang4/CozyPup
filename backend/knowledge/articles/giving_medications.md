## Why route matters
Medications can be given orally, by injection, or topically. Using the right route and the right dose is essential for the drug to be both safe and effective. The choice depends on the animal's species, size, the condition being treated, and the drug itself.

## Oral medications
Given by mouth. Common forms:
- **Tablets and capsules** — sometimes flavored or hidden in a small amount of food the pet reliably eats. Capsule shells can mask bitter tastes.
- **Liquids** — given with a dosing syringe, pointed toward the cheek pocket rather than the back of the throat to reduce the risk of aspiration.
- **Pastes** — measured with a syringe or spoon.
- **Powders** — usually mixed into food; the whole meal must be eaten to deliver the full dose, which can be a problem for pets that aren't eating well.

## Injections (parenteral)
Given by needle, usually at the clinic. Routes include intravenous (into a vein), intramuscular, and subcutaneous (under the skin). Some chronic conditions require home injections after training from the vet.

## Topical medications
Applied to the skin, eyes, ears, or mucous membranes. Some topical forms (transdermal gels, patches) are absorbed into the bloodstream. For spot-on parasite products, apply to the back of the neck where the pet cannot lick it off.

## Safety rules
- **Follow the label and the veterinarian's instructions exactly.** Do not increase the dose or frequency to "catch up" if a dose is missed — ask the vet.
- **Finish the full course** of antibiotics and other medications unless the vet tells you to stop.
- **Never give human medications** to pets without explicit veterinary approval. Some common human medications (including certain pain relievers, cold medicines, and sweeteners like xylitol) are toxic to dogs, cats, or both.
- **Keep records** of every prescription, supplement, vitamin, or herb you give — some combinations are dangerous. Share the full list at every vet visit.
- **Store medications out of reach** of pets and children.

## When to call the vet
- The pet vomits, has diarrhea, becomes lethargic, or behaves oddly after a dose.
- You missed a dose and are not sure how to proceed.
- You see a rash, swelling, or other reaction.
- The pet accidentally receives a double dose or swallows the whole bottle.
