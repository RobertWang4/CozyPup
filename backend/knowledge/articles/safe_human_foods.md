## The short answer
Most dogs and cats can safely enjoy small amounts of certain plain, unseasoned human foods as an **occasional treat — not a meal**. The core nutrition must still come from a **complete and balanced commercial diet** meeting AAFCO nutrient profiles for the pet's life stage. A widely used veterinary rule of thumb from AAHA: **treats (including human food) should make up no more than 10% of daily calories**; the other 90% should be the balanced diet.

If you aren't sure whether a food is safe — **don't give it**. When in doubt, check with your veterinarian or a pet poison helpline.

**US/Canada 24/7 helplines:**
- ASPCA Animal Poison Control Center: **(888) 426-4435**
- Pet Poison Helpline: **(855) 764-7661**

This article is the **positive list** — foods generally considered safe in moderation for healthy adult dogs and cats. For the "never feed" list (chocolate, grapes, onions, xylitol, macadamia nuts, alcohol, etc.), see the **Toxic Foods for Dogs and Cats** article.

## Plain cooked lean meats
- **Chicken, turkey, lean beef** — plain, fully cooked, boneless, skinless, **no salt, no garlic, no onion, no seasoning**, no butter or oil.
- Remove all bones, especially cooked bones (fracture and perforation risk).
- A small piece or shred as a training treat or food topper is fine for most pets.
- Keep portions small for pets with pancreatitis risk, kidney disease, or on a therapeutic diet — ask your vet first.

## Plain cooked eggs
Plain scrambled or hard-boiled egg (no salt, butter, or seasoning) in small amounts is generally well tolerated. Avoid raw egg because of *Salmonella* risk and a biotin-binding compound in raw whites.

## Plain cooked rice or pasta
Plain white or brown rice, or plain pasta, with no sauce or seasoning, is a gentle carbohydrate often recommended by veterinarians during recovery from mild digestive upset. It is a treat or short-term supportive food, not a balanced diet.

## Plain canned or cooked pumpkin
100% plain pumpkin purée (not pumpkin pie filling, which contains sugar and spices — some of which can be harmful) is a common source of soluble fiber. A spoonful mixed with food is often used to support stool quality. Keep portions small to avoid diarrhea.

## Certain fruits (small amounts, prepared properly)
- **Apple** — remove **seeds, core, and stem** (apple seeds contain cyanogenic compounds). Offer thin slices.
- **Blueberries** — a few as a treat; rich in antioxidants.
- **Watermelon** — flesh only. **Remove seeds and rind** (rind can cause GI upset or obstruction).
- **Banana** — small slices. High in sugar, so keep portions tiny.
- **Cantaloupe / honeydew** — flesh only, no rind or seeds, small pieces.

Avoid **grapes, raisins, currants** entirely — potentially fatal kidney injury in dogs (and reported in at least one cat). Avoid citrus in large amounts — acidic and can cause GI upset.

## Certain vegetables
- **Carrots** — raw or cooked, cut to a safe size to avoid choking. A good low-calorie crunchy treat.
- **Green beans** — plain, fresh, or frozen (no salt, no butter). Often used as a low-calorie filler for dogs on a weight-loss plan, **with vet guidance**.
- **Cucumber** — plain slices, no skin if waxed, no seasoning.
- **Plain cooked sweet potato or squash** — small amounts, no butter, no sugar, no marshmallows.

**Avoid** onions, garlic, leeks, chives, raw potatoes and potato plants, and raw/undercooked nightshade leaves.

## Dairy (cautious, small amounts)
Many adult dogs and most adult cats are **lactose intolerant** to some degree. If tolerated:
- **Plain cottage cheese** — small spoonful as an occasional treat or topper.
- **Plain unsweetened yogurt** — small amount; avoid any yogurt containing **xylitol**, fruit, or added sugar. Xylitol is **lethally toxic to dogs**.

If your pet gets diarrhea or gas after dairy, stop and don't give it again.

## Can dogs eat cat food? Can cats eat dog food?
- **An occasional stolen bite is usually not toxic**, but the diets are not interchangeable.
- **Cat food is too high in protein and fat for dogs long-term** and can contribute to weight gain and pancreatitis in susceptible dogs.
- **Cats eating dog food long-term will become deficient in taurine, arachidonic acid, and vitamin A**, which can cause serious heart and eye disease. Cats have higher protein requirements and specific amino acids dogs don't need.
- Feed each species its own species-appropriate, AAFCO-compliant diet. If one pet keeps raiding the other's bowl, separate them at meals.

## The 10% rule (AAHA)
The AAHA nutrition guidelines recommend that **treats and human food together make up no more than 10% of a pet's daily calories**. The remaining 90% should be a complete and balanced diet. Over-treating is one of the most common causes of pet obesity in North America.

Practical tips:
- Break treats into small pieces — dogs and cats don't care about size, they care about the event.
- Count human-food treats toward the daily total; reduce kibble slightly on generous-treat days.
- Use vegetables (carrot, green bean, cucumber) as low-calorie options for pets watching their weight.

## When to skip human food altogether
- Pets on a **prescription (therapeutic) diet** — off-diet treats undo the benefit.
- Pets with **food allergies, pancreatitis, kidney disease, diabetes, or IBD** — talk to your vet first.
- **Puppies and kittens** under about 6 months — stick close to their balanced growth diet.
- Pregnant or lactating pets — balanced nutrition matters more than variety.

## When to see a veterinarian
- Vomiting, diarrhea, lethargy, or loss of appetite after trying a new food
- Suspected ingestion of a toxic food (chocolate, grapes/raisins, onion/garlic, xylitol, macadamia, alcohol) — **this is an emergency**, call a vet or poison helpline immediately
- Unexplained weight gain, weight loss, or body condition changes
- Chronic itching, ear infections, or digestive upset that could be food-related — diagnosis needs a **veterinary-supervised elimination diet**, not a blood or saliva "allergy test"
