## Why this matters
Many human medications — both over-the-counter and prescription — are dangerous to dogs and cats, sometimes at very small doses. **Never give a human medication to a pet without explicit veterinary approval.** Accidental ingestion (a dropped pill, a chewed bottle, a pet getting into a bag) is also one of the most common reasons pets end up in emergency hospitals.

If your pet has eaten any human medication — prescription or OTC — **treat it as an emergency** and call for help immediately. Outcomes depend on how quickly you act.

**US/Canada 24/7 helplines:**
- ASPCA Animal Poison Control: **(888) 426-4435**
- Pet Poison Helpline: **(855) 764-7661**

## High-risk OTC medications

### NSAIDs — ibuprofen, naproxen, aspirin
Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs are the most commonly accidentally ingested human medications in pets. They have a **narrow safety margin** in dogs and an even narrower margin in cats, which are very sensitive.
- **Ibuprofen** (Advil, Motrin): can cause vomiting, diarrhea, GI ulcers and perforations, kidney damage, CNS depression, seizures, coma, and death.
- **Naproxen** (Aleve): similar to ibuprofen but even more likely to cause GI bleeding. Signs include black stool, vomiting, weakness, pale gums, anemia.
- **Aspirin**: cats are especially sensitive and can die from large doses. Dogs tolerate it better but long-term use causes ulcers.

### Acetaminophen (Tylenol, paracetamol)
**Extremely toxic to cats** — even a single regular-strength tablet can be fatal. Cats develop life-threatening anemia (methemoglobinemia), brown discoloration of blood and gums, facial swelling, difficulty breathing, and liver failure. Dogs are also affected but usually need higher doses; signs include liver damage, brown mucous membranes, and respiratory distress.

### Antihistamines (Benadryl, Claritin, Zyrtec, etc.)
Active ingredients: diphenhydramine, chlorpheniramine, loratadine, cetirizine, meclizine, and similar. Overdose signs include drooling, depression or hyperactivity, rapid heart rate, tremors, incoordination, fever, and seizures. Some combination cold/flu products also contain decongestants or acetaminophen, multiplying the risk.

### Decongestants — pseudoephedrine, phenylephrine, imidazolines
Found in cold medicines, nasal sprays, and eye drops. Can cause agitation, enlarged pupils, rapid heartbeat, high blood pressure, tremors, vomiting, seizures, and death. Imidazoline eye/nose sprays (oxymetazoline, tetrahydrozoline) are especially dangerous because small volumes contain high concentrations.

### Iron and multivitamins
High-dose iron tablets (prenatal vitamins, iron supplements) cause vomiting (sometimes bloody), lethargy, shock, liver damage, and cardiovascular collapse. Vitamin D in some supplements is also toxic in overdose.

### Topical zinc oxide (diaper cream, sunscreens)
Licking diaper rash creams off skin causes drooling and vomiting; larger ingestions can cause anemia in dogs.

## High-risk prescription medications
Accidental ingestion of a family member's prescription is a common emergency. Particularly dangerous classes include:
- **ADHD medications (amphetamines, methylphenidate)** — rapid heart rate, hyperthermia, seizures, death.
- **Antidepressants (SSRIs, SNRIs, TCAs)** — "serotonin syndrome" with tremors, hyperthermia, agitation, seizures.
- **Sleep aids and anti-anxiety (benzodiazepines, zolpidem)** — sedation, incoordination, paradoxical excitation.
- **Beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers, ACE inhibitors** — low blood pressure, slow heart rate, collapse.
- **Diabetes medications (insulin, sulfonylureas)** — dangerous low blood sugar, seizures.
- **Opioid pain medications** — sedation, respiratory depression, death.
- **Birth control patches and testosterone gels** — hormonal effects, especially in small pets that chew a discarded patch.

## Other common pet poisoning sources worth knowing
- **Marijuana / cannabis edibles** — increasingly common; often combined with chocolate or xylitol, compounding the risk.
- **Nicotine** (cigarettes, vape cartridges, nicotine gum, nicotine patches).
- **Essential oils** (especially tea tree, pennyroyal, pine, citrus) — cats are highly sensitive via skin contact or inhalation.
- **Rodenticides and insecticides** — many different types, different antidotes; always bring the packaging.
- **Antifreeze (ethylene glycol)** — sweet-tasting, extremely lethal, small amounts can kill.

## Signs a pet may have been poisoned
- Vomiting, diarrhea (possibly with blood)
- Drooling, foaming at the mouth
- Weakness, wobbly gait, collapse
- Tremors or seizures
- Abnormal breathing — rapid, slow, or labored
- Pale, blue, brown, or yellow gums
- Unusual sedation or agitation
- Enlarged pupils, disorientation

## When to seek veterinary care
**Immediately** — call ahead so the clinic is ready — if any of these are true:
- You saw or suspect your pet ate a medication or household chemical
- Your pet is showing any signs listed above without a clear cause
- The pet is a cat and the medication is acetaminophen (even one tablet is an emergency)
- The substance is unknown

Do not "wait and see." Many poisons cause damage that is much easier to prevent than to reverse.

## What to do right now
1. **Move the pet away** from more of the substance. Take the wrapper/bottle with you.
2. **Call a vet or pet poison helpline.** Report species, age, weight, what was eaten, how much, and when. If you don't know the amount, say so — they will plan for worst case.
3. **Do not induce vomiting** unless instructed. For caustic substances, sharp objects, and symptomatic pets, vomiting causes more harm.
4. **Bring the product, packaging, or a photo of the label** to the clinic — active ingredients and strength are critical.

## Prevention
- Store all medications — including supplements and topicals — in closed cabinets out of reach.
- **Never leave pill bottles on nightstands**, bags on the floor, or daily pill organizers on counters.
- Dispose of empty bottles and used patches where pets cannot access them.
- Tell guests and visiting family to stash medications.
- Ask the vet before giving any OTC product — if approval is given, get the exact dose, frequency, and duration.
