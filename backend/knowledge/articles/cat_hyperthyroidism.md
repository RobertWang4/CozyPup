## What it is
**Hyperthyroidism** is one of the most common hormonal disorders in older cats. A usually benign growth in the thyroid gland produces too much thyroid hormone, which revs up the cat's metabolism. It most often affects cats over 10 years old.

Because it is a disease of older cats, it frequently appears alongside other conditions (kidney disease, high blood pressure, heart disease), and treating one condition can change how others behave — so ongoing veterinary monitoring matters.

## Signs to watch for
Classic picture: an older cat with an **enormous appetite but losing weight**.

Common signs:
- **Weight loss**, often noticeable even with a good or increased appetite
- **Increased appetite** (hyperphagia)
- **Increased thirst and urination**
- **Hyperactivity, restlessness, or changes in temperament** — some cats become more irritable or vocal, especially at night
- **A rough or unkempt coat**
- **Vomiting or diarrhea**, larger stools
- **Rapid heart rate or breathing**
- Over time, **muscle loss** and weakness

Some cats instead become **quiet, withdrawn, and poor eaters** — this is called "apathetic" hyperthyroidism and is less common but worth recognizing.

## Why it matters
Untreated hyperthyroidism strains the heart, raises blood pressure, and can damage other organs. It can also **mask underlying kidney disease**, which may only become apparent once the thyroid is treated. That's why cats with this diagnosis need careful, ongoing monitoring.

## When to see the vet
- Your senior cat is losing weight even though it eats well
- Your cat drinks or urinates more than usual, vomits frequently, or becomes unusually active or vocal
- You feel a lump in the front of the neck
- Routine wellness — any cat over about 7 years old should have periodic bloodwork and thyroid screening, even without symptoms

## What the vet may do
Diagnosis typically involves a physical exam (the vet may feel an enlarged thyroid), bloodwork including **total T4 (thyroid hormone)**, and sometimes additional thyroid tests if the T4 is borderline. Because hyperthyroid cats often have or will develop other issues, the vet will also check kidney values, blood pressure, and sometimes heart function.

Treatment options vary and are chosen based on the individual cat:
- **Medication** (daily oral or transdermal) to lower thyroid hormone
- **Prescription iodine-restricted diet** (only works if the cat eats nothing else)
- **Radioactive iodine therapy (I-131)** — often curative; available at specialty centers
- **Surgery** to remove the affected thyroid tissue — less common today

Each option has pros, cons, and monitoring requirements. The vet will discuss what fits the cat and the household.

## Home care
- Give medications exactly as prescribed — missed doses can undo control.
- Keep all follow-up bloodwork appointments; dose adjustments are common.
- Track weight, appetite, water intake, litter box use, and activity. Written notes help the vet catch changes early.
- Report new signs — increased thirst, appetite loss, vomiting, or breathing changes — without waiting for the next appointment.
