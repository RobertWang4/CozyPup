## What it is
Otitis externa is inflammation of the lining of the outer ear canal — the most common ear disorder in dogs. It is **not** the same as ear mites; in otitis externa the underlying problem is usually bacteria, yeast (most often *Malassezia*), allergies, water in the ears, foreign material, or a combination. A normal ear has small numbers of harmless microbes; disease develops when the ear environment changes and these organisms overgrow.

Ear mites can coexist with or trigger otitis externa, but by itself "bacterial/yeast otitis externa" is a distinct problem and needs different treatment from mites alone.

## Why it happens
- **Allergies** (food or environmental) — by far the most common underlying cause of recurring ear infections in dogs
- **Anatomy** — floppy ears (Cocker Spaniels, Basset Hounds, Labradors), narrow canals, or hairy canals trap moisture and debris
- **Moisture** — frequent swimming or bathing
- **Foreign material** — grass seeds (especially foxtails), trapped hair
- **Ear mites** (more common in puppies; can coexist with bacterial/yeast infection)
- **Underlying skin disease** — seborrhea, atopy, endocrine disease
- **Middle ear disease** extending outward

Unless the underlying cause is treated, otitis externa tends to come back.

## Signs to watch for
- Head shaking, tilting, or rubbing the ear on furniture
- Scratching at one or both ears
- Foul or yeasty smell from the ear (a sharp sweet smell often means yeast)
- Dark brown, black, yellow, or pus-like discharge
- Redness and swelling of the ear flap or canal opening
- Pain when the ear or base of the ear is touched — yelping, flinching, snapping
- Hair loss and scabs on the ear flap from scratching
- In severe/chronic cases: a thickened, almost rubbery ear canal; hearing loss; balance problems (suggests middle-ear involvement)

## Questions to think about before seeing the vet
- One ear or both?
- When did it start, and has this dog had ear problems before?
- Any recent swimming, bathing, or grooming?
- Any skin itching elsewhere (paws, belly, groin)?
- Any change in diet or environment?
- What color/consistency is the discharge?

## When to seek veterinary care
Book a visit when the ear is visibly discharging, smelly, red, or painful, or when the dog is shaking its head repeatedly. Don't delay — ear disease becomes harder and more expensive to treat the longer it is left, and chronic cases can permanently damage the ear canal.

Seek **urgent care** for sudden severe pain, a head tilt, drooping face, circling, or loss of balance — these can indicate the infection has reached the middle or inner ear.

## What the vet may do
- Examine the ear with an otoscope (sometimes under sedation if the ear is too painful)
- Take a swab of discharge for **cytology** under a microscope — this is how the vet tells bacteria from yeast from mites
- Culture the discharge in severe or recurrent cases
- Check for foreign bodies, ruptured eardrum, or masses in the canal
- Clean the ear thoroughly (sometimes under anesthesia)
- Prescribe a topical ear medication matched to what was found on the swab, often combining an antibiotic, antifungal, and anti-inflammatory
- Add oral medication when the infection is deep or severe
- Investigate and treat the **underlying cause** — this might mean allergy testing, a diet trial, or treating a skin condition
- Schedule rechecks (commonly 2–4 weeks) to make sure the infection has fully cleared, not just improved

## Home care guidance
- **Follow the prescribed course exactly** and finish the full treatment even if the ear looks better early. Stopping early is the main reason infections recur.
- Clean only as directed by the veterinarian, with the product they recommend. Over-cleaning can worsen inflammation.
- **Do not use vinegar, hydrogen peroxide, alcohol, rubbing alcohol, or home-remedy mixtures** in an inflamed ear — these can burn the canal and worsen the problem.
- **Never use cotton swabs (Q-tips)** deep in the canal — they push debris down against the eardrum.
- Keep water out of the ears during bathing; for dogs that swim, use a veterinarian-approved ear-drying solution after swimming.
- For recurrent ear issues, work with the veterinarian on the underlying cause — repeated courses of medication without addressing allergies or anatomy will not solve the problem.
- Never share ear medication between pets, or use a leftover prescription from a prior infection — different infections need different drugs, and the wrong product can make things worse, especially if the eardrum is ruptured.
