## Why this matters
Pets — especially curious puppies and kittens, and pets that are bored or newly in a home — often chew on houseplants and ornamentals. Some common plants are harmless; others can cause serious organ damage or death. If your pet may have chewed or eaten a plant, **assume it is a problem until the specific plant is cleared**, and call a veterinarian or pet poison helpline.

**US/Canada 24/7 helplines:**
- ASPCA Animal Poison Control: **(888) 426-4435**
- Pet Poison Helpline: **(855) 764-7661**

## Especially dangerous to cats
- **Lilies (true lilies — Lilium and Hemerocallis / daylilies):** Easter lily, tiger lily, daylily, Asiatic, Oriental, Stargazer, and Japanese Show lilies can cause **acute kidney failure in cats**, even from a tiny amount — licking pollen off fur, chewing a leaf, or drinking vase water can be fatal. **All parts** of the plant are toxic. **This is a veterinary emergency.**
- **Lily-of-the-valley** — contains cardiac glycosides; affects the heart.
- **Sago palm (Cycas, Zamia)** — all parts are toxic; seeds are worst. Causes severe liver failure. High fatality rate.

## Plants commonly implicated in dog and cat poisoning
- **Azalea / rhododendron** — vomiting, diarrhea, cardiac effects, collapse.
- **Oleander** — cardiac glycosides; severe heart effects.
- **Yew** — sudden death from cardiac toxicity.
- **Foxglove (Digitalis)** — cardiac toxicity.
- **Autumn crocus (Colchicum), Crocus (spring, smaller concern)** — severe digestive and organ effects.
- **Tulips and hyacinths (especially bulbs)** — digestive upset, more severe with large bulb ingestion.
- **Daffodils / narcissus (especially bulbs)** — vomiting, diarrhea, heart and respiratory effects.
- **Castor bean (Ricinus)** — ricin; highly toxic.
- **Kalanchoe** — cardiac glycosides.
- **Dieffenbachia, philodendron, pothos, peace lily, calla lily** — oral/throat irritation from calcium oxalate crystals; drooling, pawing at mouth, sometimes swelling.
- **English ivy, schefflera, jade plant** — digestive upset.
- **Tomato, potato (green parts), onion/garlic plants** — varying digestive and systemic effects.
- **Marijuana / cannabis plants and products** — neurologic signs; increasingly common accidental exposure.

## Outdoor and seasonal hazards
- **Spring bulbs** — tulip, daffodil, hyacinth, iris.
- **Holiday plants** — mistletoe (especially European), holly, amaryllis, lilies in bouquets (cats), poinsettia (usually mild irritation rather than true poisoning).
- **Mushrooms** — wild mushroom identification is difficult; any unknown mushroom ingestion should be treated as potentially serious.
- **Compost** — mycotoxins can cause tremors and seizures.
- **Cocoa-bean-hull mulch** — chocolate toxicity.

## Signs to watch for
Signs vary by plant but may include:
- Drooling, foaming, pawing at the mouth
- Vomiting, diarrhea (may be bloody)
- Lethargy, weakness, collapse
- Tremors or seizures
- Irregular heartbeat, difficulty breathing
- Reduced or no urine output (kidney failure — lilies, grapes)
- Yellowing of skin or eye whites (liver failure — sago palm, xylitol)

## When to seek veterinary care
**Call a veterinarian or poison helpline immediately** if:
- You saw your pet chew or eat any plant you are not 100% sure is safe
- You suspect lily exposure in a cat (any amount, any part)
- You found chewed leaves, bulbs, seeds, or mushrooms and don't know what the pet ate
- Any symptoms from the list above appear

Earlier treatment is much more effective than later treatment.

## What to do at home before you reach the vet
- Try to **identify the plant** — photo of the whole plant, close-up of leaves/flowers, and a piece in a bag. A plant ID app can help but confirm with poison control.
- Note **how much** was eaten and **when**.
- Remove any remaining plant material from the pet's mouth.
- **Do not induce vomiting** without veterinary direction — for some plants (sharp crystals, corrosives) vomiting causes more harm.
- Bring the plant sample with you to the clinic.

## Prevention
- Before bringing home a new plant, check it against the ASPCA toxic plant list.
- **No true lilies in a home with cats** — this is the single most important rule for cat households.
- Hang plants out of reach, or put them in a room the pet cannot access.
- Watch carefully around holidays: lilies, amaryllis, mistletoe, poinsettia.
- Keep potting soil (some contains cocoa hulls) away from dogs.
- Supervise outdoor exploration; fence off sago palm, oleander, and yew in yards.
