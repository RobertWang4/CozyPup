## What counts as an emergency
Situations that need **immediate** veterinary care include:
- **Trauma** — hit by a car, fall, animal attack
- **Severe bleeding** that does not slow with pressure
- **Difficulty breathing**, blue or pale gums
- **Collapse, unresponsive, weak**
- **Seizure** (especially if longer than 3 minutes or more than one in 24 hours)
- **Known or suspected toxin exposure**
- **Heatstroke**
- **Straining to urinate with little/no urine**, especially a male cat
- **Bloating with non-productive retching**, especially in large dogs
- **Difficult labor** — straining more than an hour without delivering, green discharge without a puppy/kitten
- **Prolapsed organs**
- **Open wounds exposing bone**
- **Eye emergencies** — proptosis, sudden blindness, chemical splash

## Approach with care
- **Stop any ongoing hazard first** (loose dog, traffic, another animal).
- An injured pet may bite or scratch from pain/fear — **even a normally gentle one**.
- For dogs without facial injury or breathing difficulty, a makeshift muzzle (fabric strip, slip lead) is safer for handling. **Never muzzle** a pet that is vomiting, having breathing trouble, or has a face/neck injury.
- For cats, a towel wrap or a secure box reduces stress and injury risk during transport.

## Transport
- Use a **carrier or rigid flat surface** (board, cookie sheet, stiff cardboard) for injured pets.
- **Keep warm** with a blanket — shocky pets lose heat quickly.
- **Call ahead** so the clinic is ready.
- Bring: medical history, medications, vaccine records, and packaging of anything ingested.
- Report species, age, weight, what happened, and the pet's current condition.

## Prepare before emergencies
- **Save these numbers** now in your phone:
  - Your regular veterinarian
  - Nearest 24-hour emergency clinic
  - **ASPCA Animal Poison Control: (888) 426-4435**
  - **Pet Poison Helpline: (855) 764-7661**
- Know the **route** to your closest after-hours emergency hospital.
- **Basic first-aid kit**: gauze, non-stick pads, self-adhering wrap, digital thermometer, tweezers, styptic powder, saline, an appropriately-sized muzzle, a towel, a carrier.
- Record **normal vitals** when your pet is healthy: resting heart rate, resting breathing rate, gum color — deviations are much easier to spot against a baseline.
