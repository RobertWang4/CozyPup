## Why this matters
Urinary problems are common in dogs and range from uncomfortable but treatable (a simple bladder infection) to rapidly life-threatening (a male dog with a complete urethral obstruction). Signs can look similar across causes, so a dog that is straining, leaking, or passing bloody urine needs a veterinary exam — often urgently. **A male dog that cannot pass urine at all is a true emergency**, just like a blocked cat: without treatment, the bladder can rupture or the pet can develop fatal electrolyte and kidney changes within 24 hours.

## Common causes
- **Urinary tract infection (UTI)** — bacterial infection of the bladder; more common in female dogs, older dogs, and dogs with diabetes, Cushing's disease, or kidney disease.
- **Bladder stones (uroliths)** — minerals that crystallize into stones; cause irritation, bleeding, and, if one lodges in the urethra, obstruction. Some breeds (Dalmatians, Miniature Schnauzers, Bichons, Shih Tzus) are predisposed.
- **Crystals / sterile cystitis** — inflammation of the bladder without infection.
- **Prostate disease (male dogs)** — benign prostatic hyperplasia, prostatitis, or prostatic cysts, especially in intact older males. Can cause straining, bloody urine or bloody discharge, and difficulty urinating or defecating.
- **Kidney disease** — particularly in senior dogs; signs include increased thirst and urination, dilute urine, weight loss, and poor appetite.
- **Trauma** — a blow to the abdomen, being hit by a car, or a fall can injure the bladder or urethra.
- **Tumors** — transitional cell carcinoma and other bladder/urethral tumors, usually in older dogs, can mimic a stubborn UTI.
- **Congenital problems** — ectopic ureters, recessed vulva, and other anatomic issues cause recurrent infections and dribbling, especially in young female dogs.

## Signs to watch for
- **Bloody urine (hematuria)** — pink, red, orange, or brown urine, or visible clots.
- **Straining to urinate** — posturing for a long time with little or no urine produced.
- **Frequent small amounts** — trips outside every few minutes, tiny puddles.
- **Accidents in the house** in a previously house-trained dog.
- **Dribbling or leaking urine**, wet spots where the dog has been lying.
- **Licking the genital area** more than usual.
- **Strong-smelling or cloudy urine.**
- **Pain signs** — whimpering during urination, hunched posture, reluctance to walk.
- **Systemic signs** that suggest a complete blockage or kidney involvement: vomiting, loss of appetite, lethargy, collapse, a hard painful belly.

## When it is an emergency
Go to an emergency vet **right now**, any time of day or night, if:
- A male dog is straining and producing **no urine at all**, or only a few drops, for more than a few hours.
- The belly is hard, distended, or painful.
- The dog is vomiting, weak, collapsed, or unresponsive along with urinary signs.
- There is visible heavy bleeding from the urinary opening.
- The dog was recently hit by a car, fell, or took a hard blow to the belly and is now straining, bloody, or not urinating.

A complete urinary blockage can be fatal within 24 hours. Do **not** "wait until morning."

## What the vet may do
Workup for urinary issues typically includes:
- **Physical exam**, including rectal exam in male dogs to feel the prostate.
- **Urinalysis** (ideally a cystocentesis sample drawn with a sterile needle).
- **Urine culture and sensitivity** if infection is suspected.
- **Bloodwork** to check kidney values, blood sugar, and signs of infection.
- **Imaging** — X-rays and/or abdominal ultrasound to look for stones, thickened bladder wall, prostate changes, and masses.
- For a blocked dog: emergency stabilization, relief of the obstruction (catheterization or, in some cases, surgery), IV fluids, pain control, and hospitalization.

Treatment depends entirely on the cause. Infections are treated with a course of antibiotics guided by culture. Stones may be dissolved with a prescription diet (some types) or removed surgically. Prostate disease in intact males often improves dramatically after neutering. Tumors are treated with a combination of medication and, sometimes, surgery or radiation. **Never give leftover antibiotics or human urinary products** — the wrong choice delays diagnosis and can make stones or resistant infections worse.

## Prevention and home care
- **Fresh water always available.** Dilute urine is protective against both infection and stone formation.
- **Frequent bathroom breaks** — holding urine for very long periods raises infection risk.
- **Weight control and regular exercise.**
- **Follow a prescribed diet** if your dog has a stone or crystal history; do not add unprescribed supplements.
- **Clean perineal area** in long-coated or skin-folded breeds.
- **Routine wellness exams**, especially for senior dogs — a urinalysis once or twice a year catches early kidney and bladder disease.
- **Track symptoms** — when did it start, color of urine, frequency, any change in thirst or appetite. Photos of abnormal urine on a paper towel are genuinely useful at the vet.

## When to see the vet (non-emergency)
Schedule a prompt visit — within 24–48 hours — if your dog is urinating more often than usual, has pinkish or cloudy urine, is leaking, is licking the genital area repeatedly, or is having house-training accidents. Recurrent UTIs or signs that keep coming back need a fuller workup; the underlying cause (stones, anatomy, endocrine disease) is often missed when only the symptom is treated.
