## What it is
Diarrhea in cats is loose or liquid stool, often more frequent than normal. It is a sign of an underlying issue rather than a disease on its own. Common causes include diet changes or intolerance, parasites, stress, infections (viral, bacterial, protozoal such as *Giardia* or *Tritrichomonas*), inflammatory bowel disease, hyperthyroidism, organ disease, pancreatitis, food allergies, foreign material, and toxin exposure.

## Acute vs. chronic
- **Acute diarrhea** — sudden onset, usually less than 2 weeks. Often self-limiting.
- **Chronic diarrhea** — persists or recurs over weeks. Needs a diagnostic workup. Chronic diarrhea is particularly worth investigating in cats because it can signal serious conditions.

## What to watch for and note
- Stool consistency, color, and frequency
- Blood, mucus, worms, or foreign material in the stool
- Straining in the litter box vs. accidents outside it
- Vomiting, decreased appetite, hiding, lethargy, fever, weight loss
- Dehydration signs: tacky gums, lack of skin elasticity, sunken eyes
- Recent diet change, new treats, new medications, or access to plants/toxins
- Indoor/outdoor status; exposure to other cats; flea and parasite prevention

## When to seek veterinary care
- Diarrhea lasts more than 24–48 hours, or repeats frequently
- Blood or a black tarry appearance in the stool
- The cat is weak, not eating, hiding, visibly dehydrated, or repeatedly vomiting
- A kitten with diarrhea — dehydration happens fast
- A senior cat, or a cat with kidney disease, hyperthyroidism, or diabetes
- Multiple cats in the home develop diarrhea at the same time

## What the vet may do
Common diagnostic steps: history and physical exam, fecal testing for parasites (including *Giardia*), bloodwork (including thyroid in older cats), urinalysis, and sometimes X-rays, ultrasound, or endoscopy with biopsy for chronic cases. Treatment addresses the underlying cause and may include deworming, diet change, probiotics, fluids for dehydration, and targeted medications.

## Home care guidance
For mild short-term diarrhea in an otherwise well adult cat, the vet may suggest a brief dietary rest followed by small, bland meals. **Always keep water available** — do not restrict water without veterinary direction. Do not give human anti-diarrhea medications to cats; several are toxic. Keep litter boxes clean and separate multiple cats if one is unwell.
