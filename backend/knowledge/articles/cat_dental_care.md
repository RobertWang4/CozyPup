## What it is
Dental disease is one of the most common health issues in cats, and it is usually underdiagnosed because cats hide mouth pain so well. Plaque — a sticky film of bacteria — forms on the teeth daily. If it is not removed, it hardens into **tartar**, which inflames the gums (**gingivitis**) and damages the supporting structures of the tooth (**periodontitis**). Cats are also prone to a cat-specific problem called **tooth resorption**, where the body slowly dissolves the tooth from within — it is painful and common.

## Signs of dental problems
- **Bad breath** (the most common early sign — "kitty breath" is not normal)
- **Yellow or brown buildup** along the gumline
- **Red, swollen, or bleeding gums**
- **Drooling**, sometimes with blood
- **Chattering or crying** when eating, dropping food, or chewing on one side
- **Decreased appetite**, choosing soft food over kibble, weight loss
- **Pawing at the mouth** or rubbing the face on surfaces
- **Head shaking**, hiding, or behavior changes
- **Loose, broken, or discolored teeth**; holes along the gumline (a sign of resorption)
- A facial swelling below the eye can signal a tooth root abscess

Many cats keep eating despite significant pain, so the absence of appetite loss does **not** rule out dental disease.

## When to see the vet
- Any of the signs above
- Your cat has never had a dental exam, or hasn't had one in the past year
- Your cat is over 3 years old (dental disease is very common by this age)
- Breeds prone to dental disease (Persians, Siamese, Abyssinians) should be examined especially closely
- You notice a broken tooth, loose tooth, or visible swelling

## What the vet may do
A complete dental evaluation requires general anesthesia, because the full mouth, including the area below the gumline, needs to be examined and cleaned properly. A veterinary dental procedure typically includes:
- Scaling above and below the gumline
- Probing and charting each tooth
- **Full-mouth dental X-rays** (most of each tooth is under the gum; this is how tooth resorption is detected)
- Polishing
- Extraction of teeth that cannot be saved, under pain control

"Anesthesia-free dental cleaning" only removes visible surface tartar and does not address disease below the gumline, so it is not a substitute for a veterinary dental procedure — and for cats, it often misses resorption, which is painful.

## Home care
The goal is to remove plaque before it turns into tartar. Options that work for cats include:
- **Toothbrushing** with a cat-safe toothpaste — never human toothpaste (fluoride and xylitol are unsafe). Start young if possible; many adult cats can still learn with patient conditioning.
- **Veterinary Oral Health Council (VOHC)-accepted** dental diets, treats, and water additives — look for the VOHC seal.
- **Avoid very hard chews and bones**, which can fracture teeth.
- Regular wellness exams so the veterinarian can grade dental disease and recommend cleaning before it advances.
