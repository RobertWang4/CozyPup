## The short answer
- Feed a **complete and balanced commercial diet** labeled as meeting **AAFCO** (Association of American Feed Control Officials) nutrient profiles for the pet's life stage, or tested in AAFCO feeding trials.
- Dogs and cats have **very different nutritional needs** — don't feed dog food to cats.
- **Portion-control** according to the pet's weight, body condition, and activity — not to "what the bag says" alone.
- Use **fresh water** at all times.
- When in doubt, or when choosing a prescription / therapeutic diet, follow your **veterinarian's recommendation**.

## Reading a food label
A trustworthy pet food label should include:
- **AAFCO nutritional adequacy statement** — "formulated to meet AAFCO nutrient profiles for [life stage]" or "animal feeding tests...substantiate" it meets those profiles.
- **Life stage** — growth (puppy/kitten), adult maintenance, gestation/lactation, all life stages, or senior (senior isn't an official AAFCO category but is often used).
- **Ingredients** listed by weight.
- **Guaranteed analysis** — minimum protein/fat, maximum fiber/moisture.
- **Feeding guidelines** — starting-point portion per weight. Most dogs and cats need **less** than the package suggests once adjusted to their metabolism and activity.
- **Manufacturer contact info** — a reputable brand answers questions about sourcing and quality control.

## How much to feed
**Start with label guidelines, then adjust to body condition.** Two dogs of the same weight can have very different calorie needs based on breed, spay/neuter status, and activity level.

Target a **lean body condition**:
- **Ribs**: easily felt under a thin fat cover, not visible except in very short-coated breeds
- **Waist**: visible from above (a narrowing behind the ribs)
- **Abdominal tuck**: visible from the side (belly rises from chest to hip)

Adjust portions **by 10% at a time** every couple of weeks and weigh the pet monthly.

## How often to feed
- **Puppies and kittens**: 3–4 meals per day while growing, tapering as they mature
- **Adult dogs**: usually **twice daily** is healthier than once (helps blood sugar and reduces bloat risk in large breeds)
- **Adult cats**: multiple small meals or portioned dry food; many cats do well with scheduled wet meals plus a measured amount of dry
- **Senior pets**: follow the vet's recommendation; often smaller more frequent meals

**Avoid free-feeding** (food out all day) for most dogs — it makes portions unclear and contributes to obesity. Many cats can free-feed, but only if they maintain a healthy weight.

## Transitioning to a new food
Sudden diet changes cause digestive upset. Transition **over 5–7 days**:
- Days 1–2: 75% old / 25% new
- Days 3–4: 50% old / 50% new
- Days 5–6: 25% old / 75% new
- Day 7+: 100% new

Pets with a sensitive stomach may need **10–14 days** or longer.

## Special situations

### Large- and giant-breed puppies
Need foods formulated for **large-breed growth** to prevent skeletal problems (overfeeding calcium or rapid growth can damage joints). Ask the vet.

### Overweight pets
Weight loss is the single most effective way to improve health and lifespan. Discuss a **calorie-controlled diet** and measured portions. Use a kitchen scale rather than a cup. **Don't starve** — very-low-calorie crash diets are unsafe, especially for cats (can trigger liver disease).

### Cats and water
Cats evolved to get moisture from prey. Many under-drink on dry-only diets. Offer **multiple water sources**, pet fountains, and a mix of wet and dry food — especially important for cats prone to urinary issues.

### Prescription (therapeutic) diets
Kidney, urinary, liver, intestinal, allergy, and diabetic diets are formulated to treat disease. If your vet prescribed one, **feed it exclusively** — off-diet treats can undo the benefit.

### Home-cooked and raw diets
- **Home-cooked** diets can be balanced, but **only with a veterinary nutritionist's recipe**. Random internet recipes are usually unbalanced (commonly missing calcium, essential fatty acids, taurine for cats).
- **Raw meat diets** carry real risks of *Salmonella*, *Campylobacter*, parasites, and nutritional imbalance; they are discouraged by the AVMA and AAHA, especially in households with kids, elderly, or immunocompromised people.
- **Grain-free diets** have been associated with heart disease (dilated cardiomyopathy) in some dogs; discuss with your vet before choosing one.

## Foods pets should not eat (safe "human foods" vs. hazards)

**Never feed (toxic):**
- **Chocolate**, grapes, raisins, currants
- **Onion, garlic, leeks, chives** (powdered, cooked, or raw)
- **Xylitol** (sugar-free gum, sugar-free baked goods, some peanut butters, some children's medicines)
- **Alcohol**, caffeine, energy drinks
- **Macadamia nuts**, large amounts of other nuts
- **Raw bread dough**, raw yeasted dough
- **Salty/salted human snacks**; salt dough
- **Cooked bones** — fracture and perforation risk

**Cats specifically:** also avoid lily plants, tuna-only diets (thiamine deficiency and mercury), and acetaminophen (Tylenol).

**Fine in small amounts as treats (not as a meal):**
- Plain cooked chicken or turkey (no salt, no seasoning)
- Plain cooked eggs
- Plain cooked rice or pasta
- Carrots, green beans, cucumber, blueberries
- Small pieces of plain fruit (apples without seeds, bananas, melon)

**Rule of thumb:** treats should be **no more than 10% of daily calories**. The rest should come from a balanced commercial diet.

## Food allergies and sensitivities
- Most food reactions in pets are to a **protein** (beef, chicken, dairy, egg) or sometimes a grain.
- Signs: chronic itching (ears and paws especially), recurrent ear infections, chronic digestive upset.
- Diagnosis is **not by blood or saliva "allergy tests"** — those are unreliable in dogs and cats. The only reliable method is a **veterinary-supervised elimination diet trial** of at least 8 weeks with a novel or hydrolyzed protein.

## When to ask the vet
- Unintended weight loss or gain
- Persistent digestive upset, skin/ear issues
- Questions about switching to a therapeutic diet
- Pregnancy / lactation / growing puppies and kittens
- Senior pets — nutritional needs shift with kidney, thyroid, and dental status
- Any pet with a diagnosed disease — nutrition is often part of treatment
