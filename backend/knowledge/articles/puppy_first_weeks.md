## When to bring a puppy home
Most puppies can leave their mother at about **7–8 weeks old**, once they are eating on their own and have had early socialization with mom and littermates. Puppies need a lot of attention in the first months: vet care, feeding, socialization, and training.

## First-week checklist

### Vet visit
- Schedule a first visit within the **first 1–2 weeks** of bringing the puppy home.
- The vet will do a physical exam, discuss a vaccine schedule, start parasite prevention, check a stool sample for worms, and discuss diet, socialization, and training.
- **Bring any paperwork** from the breeder, shelter, or previous vet — vaccines, deworming, microchip, parents' health info.

### Vaccinations
Puppies inherit some immunity from their mother, which fades during the first few months. Vaccines are given in a series (usually every 3–4 weeks until about 16 weeks old) so that protection takes over as maternal antibodies decline. Rabies is added according to age and local law.

**Until the series is complete, keep the puppy away from unvaccinated dogs or places used by dogs of unknown vaccine status** (dog parks, busy sidewalks in some areas). Safer options during that window: carrying the puppy, visiting vaccinated friends' homes, and attending well-run puppy classes that require proof of vaccination.

### Parasite control
Puppies commonly inherit roundworms and hookworms from their mother. Vets typically deworm on a schedule and recheck the stool every 2–4 weeks until two negative tests in a row. Monthly heartworm/flea prevention is started at the veterinarian's recommendation.

### Nutrition
- Use a **complete and balanced puppy food** labeled by AAFCO (Association of American Feed Control Officials) as appropriate for growth or all life stages.
- **Large- and giant-breed puppies** need food formulated for large-breed growth to protect developing joints.
- Puppies eat more frequently than adults — usually three to four meals per day at first, tapering as they grow. The vet will advise on portions based on weight.
- Fresh water always available.
- Most dogs switch to adult food around 9–12 months; large/giant breeds often wait 18–24 months.
- **Avoid human table food**, especially the toxic items (chocolate, grapes/raisins, onions, garlic, xylitol).

### Safety / puppy-proofing
- Secure medications, cleaning products, toxic plants, grapes and chocolate, electrical cords, and small swallowable objects.
- Crate-train with short positive sessions; a crate is a safe space, not a punishment.
- Keep ID on the puppy from day one (collar + tag) and schedule a microchip at the first vet visit if not already done.

## Socialization (ages ~3 to 16 weeks — the key window)
This is the most important period for behavior. Safe, positive exposures during this window dramatically reduce the risk of fear-based problems later. **Quality beats quantity** — scared is worse than nothing.

Good exposures:
- Different people (ages, appearances, hats, glasses, mobility aids)
- Different sounds (vacuum, traffic, doorbells, household noises) at a comfortable volume
- Different surfaces (carpet, tile, grass, wood, metal)
- Gentle handling exercises — paws touched, ears handled, mouth briefly opened, body brushed
- Vaccinated, healthy, friendly dogs
- Car rides to positive destinations
- Well-run puppy socialization classes

Avoid overwhelming crowds, aggressive dogs, or negative experiences. Pair new things with small treats and play.

## Training
- **Housetraining** works best with frequent trips outside — first thing in the morning, last thing at night, after meals, after naps, after play, and after big drinks. Praise and reward the moment the puppy goes.
- Teach basic cues: sit, down, come, stay, leave-it, loose-leash walking.
- **Positive reinforcement** (rewards, praise) is the recommended approach. Punishment-based methods tend to create fear and undermine learning.
- Short, frequent sessions (a few minutes at a time) work better than long ones.
- Puppy classes that start as early as 8 weeks (with proof of first vaccines) are an excellent combined socialization + training opportunity.

## When to call the vet right away
- Repeated vomiting or diarrhea, especially with blood
- Lethargy, not eating, hiding
- Pale or yellow gums
- Coughing, difficulty breathing
- Seizures, tremors, or sudden collapse
- Swollen belly
- Suspected toxin exposure (any listed in our toxic-foods and plants articles)
- Any trauma, bite wound, or severe limp

Puppies can decline fast, so **call sooner rather than later**.
