## What it is
Heatstroke is life-threatening overheating — the body's core temperature rises past what normal cooling can handle, and organs start to fail. Dogs and cats sweat very little; they cool mainly by **panting**. Once humidity is high or access to shade, water, and airflow is cut off, overheating can happen in **minutes**, not hours.

Normal dog/cat body temperature is roughly 100–102.5°F (38–39.2°C). Heatstroke begins around **104°F (40°C)** and becomes critical above 106°F (41°C).

## Pets at highest risk
- **Brachycephalic (flat-faced) breeds** — Bulldogs, French Bulldogs, Pugs, Boston Terriers, Boxers, Persians — their airways are inefficient at cooling.
- **Very young and very old** pets.
- **Overweight pets**.
- **Pets with heart or respiratory disease**.
- **Thick or dark coats**.
- Pets exercising or playing hard in the heat.
- **Any pet left in a parked car** — on an 85°F (29°C) day, interior temperatures can exceed 100°F (38°C) within 10 minutes and 120°F (49°C) within 35 minutes, even with windows cracked.

## Signs to recognize
Early:
- **Very heavy panting**, drooling, bright red gums
- **Restlessness** or distress
- Rapid heart rate

Progressing to emergency:
- **Skin hot to the touch**
- **Vomiting, sometimes with blood**; diarrhea
- **Stumbling, incoordination**
- **Collapse, seizures, loss of consciousness**
- Gums going from bright red to pale, bluish, or muddy

## Emergency action (do this on the way to the vet)
1. **Get out of the heat.** Move to shade or air-conditioned space immediately.
2. **Start cooling now, gently.** Apply **cool (not ice-cold) water** to the body, especially the belly, inner thighs, armpits, and paws. A soaked towel + a fan works well. Garden hose on cool, not cold.
3. **Do NOT submerge in ice water.** Rapid shock cooling can constrict blood vessels and make things worse.
4. **Offer small sips of cool water** if the pet is conscious and able to swallow. Never force water into a collapsing pet.
5. **Stop active cooling at ~103°F (39.4°C)** — further cooling can tip the pet into hypothermia.
6. **Go to the veterinarian.** Even a pet who looks recovered can develop delayed organ injury — kidney failure, clotting problems, brain swelling — over the following 24–72 hours. **This is not optional.**

## Not the same thing: malignant hyperthermia
A separate, rare condition called **malignant hyperthermia** is a genetic muscle disorder triggered by certain anesthetics, stress, or exertion — it causes body temperature to spike dangerously. If your dog has had any suspicious reaction to anesthesia, tell every future veterinarian before procedures.

## Prevention
- **Never leave a pet in a parked car**, even with windows cracked, even for "a minute." Cars heat up far faster than you expect.
- **Exercise in the cool parts of the day** — early morning or late evening in summer.
- **Always provide shade and fresh water** outside, and a cool surface to lie on.
- **Limit exercise for brachycephalic breeds** on warm/humid days.
- In the home, make sure pets can reach a cool room during a heat wave; check that air conditioning works.
- Watch humidity as well as temperature — 80°F at 90% humidity is more dangerous than 85°F dry.
