## What counts as trauma emergency
Any of the following needs a **veterinarian now** — call ahead and transport:
- Hit by a car, fall from a height
- Attack or bite from another animal
- Deep lacerations, punctures, or impalement
- Crushing injury, caught in machinery
- Heavy bleeding that does not slow with several minutes of pressure
- Severe limp with inability to bear weight
- Broken teeth or jaws
- Chest injury — fast, shallow, or labored breathing
- Abdominal injury — distended, painful belly, pale gums
- Head injury — wobbling, disorientation, unequal pupils, seizures
- Signs of shock: weakness, collapse, very pale or muddy gums, very fast or very slow heart rate, cool extremities

Even if the outside looks fine after an accident, **internal injuries can be delayed** — always have the vet check.

## Your safety first
An injured pet — even a gentle one — may bite or scratch from pain and fear.
- Stop any ongoing danger first (loose dog, traffic, other animal, smoke).
- If a dog has a face/mouth injury or is struggling to breathe, **do not muzzle**.
- Otherwise, a makeshift muzzle (strip of fabric, leash, gauze roll) makes handling safer.
- Cats are best wrapped in a towel ("burrito") or placed in a carrier/box.
- Never put your face near an injured pet's face.

## Controlling bleeding
1. **Apply firm direct pressure** with a clean cloth, gauze pad, or even a shirt.
2. Hold pressure for **at least 3–5 minutes without lifting to peek** — peeking restarts the clot.
3. If blood soaks through, **add more padding on top**; do not remove the underlying layer.
4. For a limb, elevating slightly above the heart can help.
5. **Tourniquets are a last resort** and can cause permanent damage; use only if instructed by a vet and only on a limb for truly uncontrolled bleeding, and tell the vet the time applied.
6. Even if bleeding stops, have the wound examined — punctures and animal bites often get infected and may need flushing, antibiotics, or stitches.

## Suspected fractures
- **Do not try to splint** at home — you can worsen the injury.
- Gently slide the pet onto a rigid surface (board, cookie sheet, stiff cardboard) for transport.
- Restrict movement. Keep the pet calm.
- Don't try to reposition a limb that is at an odd angle.

## Animal bites and puncture wounds
Animal bites look small on the surface but damage tissue below, seed bacteria, and are prone to abscess.
- Rinse with clean water or saline.
- Do **not** bandage a deep puncture tightly — it traps bacteria. Cover loosely and go.
- Bite wounds to the chest, neck, or abdomen are always more serious than they look.
- If your pet was bitten by a wild animal or an unknown pet, **rabies exposure** is a public-health issue — contact your vet and local animal control.

## Shock — what it looks like and what to do
Shock is a failure of the circulation. Signs include weakness or collapse, very pale or grayish/muddy gums, rapid or very slow heart rate, rapid shallow breathing, cool ears and paws, and low body temperature.

At the scene:
- Keep the pet **warm** with a blanket (shocky pets lose heat fast).
- Lay them on their side.
- Do **not** give food or water.
- Minimize movement.
- **Transport now** — shock worsens quickly and needs IV fluids and oxygen at a clinic.

## Lameness and suspected limb injury
- Mild limp that resolves with rest in a day or two is usually minor — restrict activity and watch.
- **Non-weight-bearing** lameness, visible deformity, open wound, or a pet in obvious pain — see a vet promptly.
- Sudden severe limp in a large/active breed can be a cruciate ligament tear or other serious injury; plan for imaging.

## Burns and caustic exposure
- Flush **cool (not cold) water** over the area for several minutes.
- Do **not** apply butter, oils, or ointments.
- Cover loosely with clean, damp cloth.
- For chemical splashes (including eye), flush with clean water or saline for 15 minutes on the way to the vet.

## Eye injury
- Cover with a clean, moist cloth; prevent rubbing (e-collar if available).
- Do **not** push a protruding eye back — cover moist and go immediately.
- Chemical splash to the eye: flush with saline or clean water for 15 minutes while transporting.

## What to bring to the clinic
- The pet in a carrier or on a rigid surface
- Any object involved (piece of a bitten item, photo of the scene, wrapper of a substance)
- Medication list and known medical conditions
- Your clearest account of what happened and when
