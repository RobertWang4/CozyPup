## What it is
Dogs and cats have a pair of small scent glands (**anal sacs**) on either side of the anus. Normally these sacs empty a small amount of strong-smelling fluid each time the pet passes stool. When the sacs don't empty properly, the fluid thickens and the sacs can become **impacted**, **inflamed (sacculitis)**, **infected**, or form an **abscess** that may rupture through the skin. Anal sac disease is one of the most common problems of the anal region in dogs. It is far less common in cats, and when cats are affected it is usually impaction.

## Why it happens
Common contributing factors include:
- The sacs not being squeezed out naturally during defecation (often with soft stool)
- Poor muscle tone in overweight or obese pets
- Excessive gland secretion, sometimes linked to generalized seborrhea or other skin disease
- Duct obstruction, bacterial overgrowth, or infection leading to abscess formation
- Small-breed dogs are predisposed; large- and giant-breed dogs are rarely affected

## Signs to watch for
- **Scooting** — dragging the rear end along the floor or carpet
- **Excessive licking or biting** at the anal area or base of the tail
- **Straining or pain while defecating**, or obvious discomfort when sitting
- A strong, unusually **fishy or foul odor** from the rear
- **Swelling, redness, or a draining sore** just beside the anus (possible abscess)
- In cats, more subtle signs: overgrooming under the tail, hiding, or avoiding the litter box

## What the vet may do
Diagnosis is usually made by a rectal exam. Depending on what is found, treatment may include:
- **Manual expression** of the impacted sacs
- **Flushing** the sacs and instilling medication for sacculitis
- **Antibiotics and anti-inflammatories** for infection; warm compresses for abscesses
- **Lancing and draining** an abscess if one has formed
- **Surgical removal of the sacs (anal sacculectomy)** for recurrent disease, non-healing infection, or a mass in the sac

Masses in the anal sac area in older dogs are not always infection — they can be tumors, which is another reason to have a vet look rather than trying to "fix it at home."

## Prevention and home care
- **Keep your pet at a lean body weight** — obesity is a major contributor.
- **Feed a complete and balanced diet.** Your veterinarian may suggest added dietary fiber to bulk up the stool so the sacs empty naturally.
- **Don't routinely express the sacs yourself unless your vet has shown you how and recommends it.** Most healthy pets never need manual expression; doing it unnecessarily can irritate the sacs.
- **Treat any underlying skin disease** (allergies, seborrhea) — chronic skin problems often go hand-in-hand with anal sac problems.

## When to see a veterinarian
Call your vet if you notice:
- Persistent scooting, licking, or a foul odor that doesn't go away after a bowel movement
- Swelling, redness, or a draining wound near the anus
- Pain when sitting or defecating, straining, or blood in the stool
- Any lump or firmness felt near the anus, especially in an older pet
- Repeated episodes — recurrent anal sac disease needs a workup, not just repeated expressions
