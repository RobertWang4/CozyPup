## What it is
Vomiting is the forceful ejection of stomach or upper small-intestine contents, usually preceded by nausea, drooling, retching, and abdominal contractions. It differs from **regurgitation**, which is a passive motion without abdominal effort and typically produces undigested, cylinder-shaped food; regurgitation is more often paired with coughing or breathing difficulty.

Causes range widely: digestive disease, kidney or liver failure, pancreatitis, nervous-system disorders, and ingestion of irritants or poisons.

## Acute vs. chronic
- **Acute (short-term):** vomiting for less than 3–4 days with no other signs. Often resolves on its own once the trigger is removed.
- **Chronic (long-term):** vomiting that continues beyond a few days, recurs more than once or twice a day, or comes with blood, abdominal pain, lethargy, dehydration, weakness, fever, or weight loss. Needs a detailed workup.

## Questions to think about before seeing the vet
- How long has the vomiting been happening, and how often?
- What does the vomit look like (food, bile, blood, foreign material)?
- Any possible access to garbage, human food, plants, medications, or toxins?
- Other signs present? (diarrhea, lethargy, appetite loss, abdominal pain, weight loss)
- Is the dog drinking? Is water staying down?

## When to seek veterinary care
Contact a veterinarian promptly if any of the following apply:
- Vomiting continues beyond 24 hours, or happens more than once or twice a day.
- Blood appears in the vomit, or the vomit looks like coffee grounds.
- The dog is weak, lethargic, feverish, painful, or visibly dehydrated.
- You suspect the dog swallowed a foreign object, a toxin, or a medication.
- The patient is a puppy, a senior, pregnant, or has known kidney, liver, or heart disease.

## What the vet may do
For short-term vomiting, the exam typically includes history, physical and abdominal exam, oral exam, rectal exam, and often X-rays to rule out a foreign object or other serious disease. Blood, urine, and fecal tests may also be run.

For chronic vomiting, additional workup can include endoscopy with biopsy, abdominal ultrasound, CT, or MRI, plus treatment for dehydration and electrolyte or acid-base imbalances. Anti-vomiting medication may be prescribed.

## Home care guidance
Any home care should follow the veterinarian's instructions for the specific case. A common approach for mild short-term vomiting is withholding food for a short period and reintroducing small amounts of easily digested food if vomiting stops — but **water should not be withheld** unless the vet has put the dog on supplemental fluids, because dehydration can make things worse. Do not give human medications without veterinary direction.
