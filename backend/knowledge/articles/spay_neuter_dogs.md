## What it is
**Spaying** is the surgical removal of a female dog's reproductive organs. **Neutering** is the surgical removal of a male dog's testicles. Both prevent reproduction. The procedures are done under general anesthesia and are among the most common surgeries in veterinary practice.

## Why it is recommended
Unless a dog is being bred intentionally and responsibly, spay/neuter is generally recommended. Benefits include:
- Preventing unplanned litters and reducing pet overpopulation.
- For females, spaying before the first heat substantially reduces the risk of mammary (breast) tumors later in life, and eliminates the risk of uterine infection (pyometra) and uterine or ovarian cancers.
- For males, neutering can reduce urine marking, roaming, and certain behavior problems, and eliminates the risk of testicular cancer.

## Timing
General guidance for pet dogs:
- **Females** are typically spayed around 6 months of age, before the first heat cycle.
- **Males** are typically neutered between about 5 and 10 months of age.

**However, the best age depends on the dog.** Large- and giant-breed dogs, dogs with certain orthopedic risks, and working dogs may benefit from waiting longer. Some breeds have specific research-based recommendations. Your veterinarian will give a personalized recommendation based on breed, size, health, and lifestyle.

## What the procedure involves
- A pre-surgical exam and often bloodwork to check that the dog is healthy for anesthesia.
- Fasting the night before, per veterinary instructions.
- General anesthesia and a surgical incision in the abdomen (females) or scrotum (males).
- A short recovery at the clinic, then home the same day or the next, depending on the hospital.
- A 10–14 day recovery period with restricted activity, an e-collar to prevent licking, and a re-check or suture removal.

## What to watch for at home after surgery
Contact the veterinarian if you notice:
- Excessive bleeding, swelling, redness, or discharge at the incision
- The incision reopening
- Vomiting, refusing food for more than 24 hours, lethargy, or pain that isn't controlled by prescribed pain medication
- Your dog licking, chewing, or scratching the incision despite the e-collar

## Common questions
- **Will my dog gain weight?** Metabolism slows slightly after spay/neuter. Adjust food portions and keep up exercise.
- **Does it change personality?** Dogs remain themselves. Certain hormone-driven behaviors (roaming, marking, mounting) may lessen in males.
- **Is it painful?** The vet uses anesthesia and sends the dog home with pain medication. Most dogs recover quickly.
