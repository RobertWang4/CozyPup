## Why this matters
Several common human foods are poisonous to dogs and cats, and some can be fatal. If your pet has eaten — or may have eaten — any of the items below, **treat it as an emergency**: contact a veterinarian or a pet poison helpline immediately. Do not wait for signs to appear. For many toxins, treatment is most effective when started before symptoms begin.

**US/Canada 24/7 helplines:**
- ASPCA Animal Poison Control Center: **(888) 426-4435** (consultation fee may apply)

## Chocolate (dogs especially)
Chocolate contains theobromine and caffeine, which are toxic to dogs. Signs usually start within 6–12 hours and can include excessive thirst, vomiting, diarrhea, restlessness, hyperactivity, tremors, seizures, increased urination, very rapid or irregular heartbeat, high blood pressure, and fever. Severe cases can be fatal.

Toxicity rises with how dark the chocolate is: **dry cocoa powder and baker's (unsweetened) chocolate are the most dangerous**, followed by dark chocolate, semisweet, and then milk chocolate. White chocolate contains very little toxin. Mulch made from cocoa-bean hulls has also caused poisoning.

**If eaten:** call a vet or poison helpline right away, ideally with the product wrapper and the dog's weight. Treatment may include induced vomiting, activated charcoal, IV fluids, and medications for tremors, heart rhythm problems, or seizures.

## Xylitol (dogs)
Xylitol is a sugar alcohol in many sugar-free gums, candies, mints, peanut and nut butters, baked goods, some children's medicines, and some dental products. In dogs, it can cause a **rapid, severe drop in blood sugar** within 30 minutes, and at higher doses **liver failure** 1–2 days later. Signs include vomiting, weakness, incoordination, depression, seizures, and coma. Yellowing of the skin or eye whites can signal liver damage.

**Cats are not reported to be affected the same way**, but exposure should still be discussed with a veterinarian.

**If eaten:** do not try to induce vomiting at home — hypoglycemia can come on too fast. Activated charcoal does not bind xylitol. Go to a veterinary hospital immediately. Monitoring and IV glucose are critical for 24+ hours; liver values are tracked for several days.

## Grapes, raisins, and tamarinds (dogs; rarely cats)
Ingestion can cause **acute kidney failure** in dogs — and it has also been reported in at least one cat. Tartaric-acid content varies in the fruit, so amounts and effects are unpredictable. In general, more than about one grape or raisin per 10 pounds (4.5 kg) of body weight may be enough to cause kidney injury in a dog. Signs usually begin within 6–12 hours: vomiting and/or diarrhea, lethargy, appetite loss, abdominal pain, weakness, dehydration, excessive thirst, tremors. Kidney failure can follow in 1–3 days and is often fatal if the pet stops producing urine.

**If eaten within the last 15–20 minutes:** vomiting may be induced by a veterinarian, followed by activated charcoal. Larger amounts or delayed presentation usually require 48 hours of IV fluids to protect the kidneys.

## Macadamia nuts (dogs)
Dogs can develop vomiting, weakness, depression, incoordination, tremors, and fever within 12 hours. Most recover within 12–48 hours with supportive care; severe cases may need fluids and temperature management. If recent and pre-symptomatic, vomiting and activated charcoal may be recommended by a veterinarian.

## Raw bread dough (yeast)
The warm stomach acts like an incubator: the dough expands and ferments, producing ethanol absorbed into the bloodstream. Signs include unproductive retching, distended belly, depression, disorientation, incoordination, dangerously low body temperature, weakness, coma, or seizures. Large dough masses can twist the stomach in certain breeds. **This is an emergency** — call a veterinarian immediately.

## Avocado
Avocado (leaves, fruit, pit, and skin) is most dangerous to **birds, rabbits, guinea pigs, horses, and fish**. Dogs and cats are generally more resistant but can still develop digestive upset; the large pit is also a foreign-body hazard. Keep avocado away from pet birds entirely.

## Other common food hazards to avoid
(Supported by the same Merck reference and additional sources within the chapter)
- **Onions, garlic, leeks, chives (allium)** — can damage red blood cells in dogs and cats; cats are especially sensitive. Cooked, raw, powdered, or dehydrated all count.
- **Alcohol** — extremely sensitive; can cause depression, low blood sugar, low body temperature, respiratory failure.
- **Caffeine** (coffee grounds, tea bags, energy drinks) — similar to chocolate toxicity.
- **Salt / salty foods / salt dough** — can cause sodium toxicity, vomiting, tremors, seizures.
- **Moldy food** — mycotoxins can cause vomiting and seizures.
- **Bones (especially cooked)** — not a toxin but a common cause of fractures, obstructions, and perforations.

## What to do if a pet has eaten something toxic
1. **Stay calm and call for help immediately.** Sooner is always better.
2. **Note:** what was eaten, how much, and when; the pet's weight; product wrapper/packaging if available.
3. **Do not induce vomiting** unless a veterinarian or a poison-control professional instructs you to. For some substances (xylitol, corrosives, sharp objects, symptomatic patients) vomiting is harmful.
4. **Transport the pet** to a veterinary clinic with the packaging or a sample of the substance.
5. If possible, **take a photo** of the label and remaining contents; this helps with dosing and treatment decisions.

## Prevention
- Store all human food, baking ingredients, sugar-free products, and medications out of reach.
- Never give grapes, raisins, chocolate, onions, garlic, alcohol, or products containing xylitol.
- Check peanut butter labels before using for medication dosing — some now contain xylitol.
- Keep compost bins and garbage cans closed.
- Inform guests and kids that "just a little" human food can be dangerous.
