## When is a dog "senior"?
It varies by size. As a rough guide:
- **Small breeds (< 20 lbs / 9 kg)**: senior around **10–12** years
- **Medium breeds**: senior around **8–10** years
- **Large breeds (> 50 lbs / 23 kg)**: senior around **7–8** years
- **Giant breeds**: senior around **6–7** years

Large and giant breeds age faster. A 6-year-old Great Dane is already a senior; a 6-year-old Chihuahua is middle-aged.

## What changes with age
- **Slower metabolism**, less exercise tolerance
- **Joint wear**, stiffness, arthritis — especially after rest or in cold weather
- **Weight changes** — some dogs gain, some lose; both deserve attention
- **Sensory decline** — cataracts, reduced hearing, reduced sense of smell
- **Kidney, liver, heart, and endocrine (thyroid, adrenal) changes** — often silent until advanced
- **Dental disease** accumulates
- **Cognitive changes** — "canine cognitive dysfunction" (similar to dementia)
- **Sleep pattern changes**, restlessness at night
- **Lumps and bumps** — some harmless (lipomas), some not

## The senior care checklist

### 1. See the vet twice a year, not once
Senior dogs should have a **full checkup every 6 months**. Problems caught early (kidney disease, heart disease, endocrine disease, tumors) are much easier to manage. Expect the vet to recommend:
- Physical exam including body condition, muscle tone, dental, eyes, lumps
- **Annual or twice-yearly bloodwork** including complete blood count, chemistry, thyroid (especially in overweight/lethargic dogs)
- **Urinalysis** — an early signal of kidney disease and many other conditions
- **Blood pressure** when indicated
- Parasite prevention and vaccines updated per lifestyle

### 2. Watch for silent signs
Things to note and raise with the vet:
- Drinking more / peeing more / peeing in the house (kidney, diabetes, Cushing's)
- Weight change in either direction
- New lumps, or existing lumps that change
- Changes in gait — slower to rise, stiffness, reluctance to jump
- Bad breath, drooling, dropping food (dental pain)
- Coughing, tiring on walks, breathing changes (heart)
- Restlessness at night, disorientation, forgetting routines (cognitive dysfunction)
- Appetite changes, vomiting, or stool changes
- Behavior change — withdrawn, irritable, more clingy, less tolerant of being touched

### 3. Joint and mobility care
- **Maintain a lean body weight** — the single most effective thing you can do for senior joints.
- **Regular low-impact exercise** — leash walks, gentle swimming, stairs only as tolerated.
- Non-slip rugs or yoga mats on hardwood/tile.
- Ramps instead of stairs or jumping into cars.
- Orthopedic bed.
- Talk to the vet about **joint supplements, physical therapy, and pain medication** — arthritis is common, often undertreated. Never give human pain relievers.

### 4. Nutrition
- Switch to a **senior-appropriate** diet if the vet recommends, usually calorie-adjusted, sometimes modified protein/phosphorus based on kidney values.
- Feed on a schedule; avoid free-feeding.
- Watch portion sizes — metabolism slows.
- Track water intake (mark the bowl) — changes matter.
- **Dental-friendly** textures or wet food may be easier to chew.

### 5. Dental care
Senior dogs often need more dental cleanings, not fewer. Dental pain is common and silent. Consider ongoing brushing and VOHC-accepted products at home.

### 6. Cognitive health
For dogs showing signs of cognitive change (night pacing, disorientation, altered interaction, house-soiling, changed sleep cycle), discuss with the vet. Options include specific diets, supplements, environmental enrichment, and sometimes medication. Catching this early is more effective than treating it late.

### 7. Home environment
- Keep layout predictable; don't rearrange furniture for a dog with fading senses.
- Nightlights for dogs whose vision is dimming.
- Separate quiet resting area away from young pets or children.
- Stairs gated if balance is a concern.
- Shorter, more frequent potty breaks.

## When to go to the vet sooner
- Sudden collapse, fainting, extreme weakness
- Seizure
- Difficulty breathing, blue or pale gums
- Bloated belly (especially in large breeds) — possible bloat/GDV, emergency
- Sudden inability to walk on a limb
- Sudden blindness or disorientation
- Blood in vomit, stool, or urine
- Stopped eating or drinking for more than a day

## A realistic note
Quality of life — interest in food, family, favorite activities, and comfort — is the goal, not lifespan at any cost. Regular check-ins with the vet about **what "a good day" looks like** help you make the best decisions at every stage.
