## What it is
Dental disease is one of the most common chronic problems in dogs. Plaque — a sticky film of bacteria — forms on the teeth continuously. If it isn't removed, it hardens into **tartar (calculus)**, which inflames the gums (**gingivitis**) and, over time, damages the structures that hold the tooth in place (**periodontitis**). Untreated dental disease can cause pain, tooth loss, and bacteria that affect the heart, liver, and kidneys.

## Signs of dental problems
- **Bad breath** (the most common early sign)
- **Yellow or brown buildup** along the gumline, especially on the upper back teeth
- **Red, swollen, or bleeding gums**
- **Drooling**, dropping food, chewing on one side, or shying away when the face is touched
- **Loose, broken, or discolored teeth**
- **Pawing at the mouth** or facial rubbing
- **Weight loss** or reduced appetite from mouth pain
- A swelling below the eye can signal a tooth root abscess

## When to see the vet
- You notice any of the signs above.
- Your dog has never had a dental exam, or hasn't had one in the last year.
- There's a broken tooth, a loose tooth, or visible swelling in the gums or face.
- Your dog is a small breed or a brachycephalic breed — these dogs tend to develop dental disease earlier and faster.

## What the vet may do
A full dental evaluation usually requires general anesthesia, because the whole mouth needs to be examined, X-rayed below the gumline, and cleaned properly. A veterinary dental cleaning typically includes:
- Scaling above and below the gumline to remove plaque and tartar
- Probing and charting each tooth
- Dental X-rays (most of the tooth is under the gum)
- Polishing
- Extraction of teeth that are too damaged to save, under pain control

"Anesthesia-free dental cleaning" only removes visible surface tartar and does not address disease below the gumline, so it is not a substitute for a veterinary dental procedure.

## Home care
The goal is to remove plaque before it turns into tartar. Effective options include:
- **Toothbrushing** with a dog-safe toothpaste (never human toothpaste — fluoride and xylitol are unsafe for dogs). Daily is ideal; several times a week still helps.
- **Veterinary Oral Health Council (VOHC)-accepted dental diets, chews, treats, and water additives** — look for the VOHC seal on the product.
- **Avoid very hard chews** (cooked bones, antlers, hard nylon bones) — they commonly fracture teeth.
- Regular wellness exams so the veterinarian can grade dental disease and recommend cleaning before it advances.
