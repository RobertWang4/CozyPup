## Overview
Coughing, sneezing, nasal discharge, and abnormal breathing are all **signs** pointing to problems in the nose, throat, windpipe, lungs, or — sometimes — in organs outside the respiratory tract (heart, chest cavity). The pattern of the signs often narrows the list of causes.

## How to describe what you're seeing
When calling a vet, note:
- **Cough**: dry vs. wet, honking, followed by gagging, triggered by excitement, position, or after drinking
- **Breathing**: rate (breaths per minute at rest), depth, obvious effort, noisy breathing, wheeze, stridor
- **Color of the gums**: pink (normal), pale, white, blue (cyanosis — emergency), brick red (heat/shock)
- **Discharge**: from nose or eyes — one side or both; clear, cloudy, yellow-green, bloody
- **Other signs**: fever, appetite loss, lethargy, vomiting, exercise intolerance, fainting
- **History**: exposure to other dogs/cats, boarding, daycare, recent kennel cough vaccine, outdoor exposure, trauma, possible foreign body (a gulped piece of food or toy)

Resting breathing rates: **a calm, sleeping dog or cat normally breathes under 30 breaths per minute**. Consistently above 40 at rest in a sleeping pet is a warning sign, especially for heart disease.

## Common causes of cough
- **Kennel cough (infectious tracheobronchitis)** — dogs; honking cough, often after boarding, daycare, or dog parks. Usually self-limiting but contagious and sometimes progresses to pneumonia.
- **Heart disease with pulmonary congestion** — often in older small-breed dogs; soft cough, exercise intolerance, higher resting breathing rate.
- **Tracheal collapse** — toy/small breeds; "goose-honk" cough, worse with excitement or leash pulling.
- **Pneumonia** — cough with lethargy, fever, appetite loss, rapid breathing.
- **Feline asthma** — cats; episodic coughing fits, wheeze, sometimes mistaken for hairballs. Can progress quickly to respiratory distress.
- **Upper airway irritation** — dust, smoke, allergies, post-nasal drip.
- **Foreign material** — inhaled grass seed, food aspiration; sudden severe cough after a specific incident.
- **Reverse sneezing** (dogs) — loud snorting bursts for 10–30 seconds. Usually not a true problem, but persistent episodes should be checked.
- **Lung tumors or metastases** — older pets; progressive signs.

## Common causes of sneezing or nasal discharge
- **Viral upper respiratory infections** (especially cats — herpes, calici); often with eye discharge too
- **Bacterial or fungal infections**
- **Dental disease** with a root abscess communicating with the nasal cavity
- **Foreign body** in the nose — often sudden, one-sided, with pawing at the face
- **Nasal tumors** — older pets; progressive one-sided discharge, sometimes bloody
- **Allergies**

## When to seek veterinary care

**Emergency — immediately**:
- Blue, gray, or purple gums or tongue
- Open-mouth breathing in a cat (nearly always an emergency)
- Severe or sudden respiratory distress, struggling to breathe
- Choking or gagging with inability to breathe
- Collapse or loss of consciousness
- Suspected trauma to the chest
- Honey/yellow frothy discharge from the nose or mouth (pulmonary edema)

**Prompt (same day)**:
- Cough lasting more than a day or two, or worsening
- Resting breathing rate persistently above 40/min in a sleeping pet
- Exercise intolerance or rapid tiring
- Nasal discharge that is yellow-green, bloody, or one-sided
- Fever (above 103°F / 39.4°C in dogs and cats)
- Reduced appetite along with respiratory signs
- A cat with any cough or wheeze — feline airways are small and can get serious fast

## What the vet may do
A workup typically includes history, physical exam, listening to heart and lungs, checking gum color and oxygenation, and usually **chest X-rays**. Depending on findings: bloodwork, bronchial wash, culture, heart ultrasound, CT, infectious disease testing. Treatment is targeted to the cause — antibiotics for bacterial infection, airway dilators and steroids for asthma, heart medications for cardiac disease, oxygen therapy for distressed patients.

## What to do at home before you get there
- **Keep the pet calm.** Stress worsens respiratory distress.
- **Do not restrain** a cat that is struggling to breathe — transport in a carrier with minimal handling.
- **Keep cool** — heat plus respiratory distress is especially dangerous.
- Avoid forcing food or water; a pet that can't breathe well may aspirate.
- Do **not** give human cough or cold medicines — several are toxic.
