## Heat cycles (for intact females)
Most female dogs have their **first heat cycle between 6 and 12 months** of age (later in large/giant breeds). Heats come roughly every 6–8 months for most breeds. A heat lasts ~2–3 weeks with visible bleeding and swelling of the vulva; the fertile window is usually in the middle of the cycle.

Notes for owners of intact females:
- Female dogs in heat attract intact males from surprising distances — keep her on a leash and indoors as much as possible.
- **Unspayed females that are not bred have a significant lifetime risk of pyometra** (uterine infection), which is a life-threatening emergency — see "When to go to the vet" below.

## Pregnancy basics
- **Gestation length**: about **63 days** from ovulation (roughly 58–68 days from breeding).
- **Confirming pregnancy**: palpation by a veterinarian around 3–4 weeks post-breeding, ultrasound from about day 21–28, X-ray from about day 45 (to count puppies and plan).
- **Nutrition**: switch to a high-quality puppy or all-life-stages food in the second half of pregnancy and throughout lactation — energy needs rise sharply.
- **Exercise**: moderate, consistent walks throughout pregnancy. Avoid strenuous exertion late in pregnancy.
- **Medications and vaccines**: do not start new medications, flea/tick products, or vaccines without veterinary approval — some are not safe in pregnancy.
- **No rough play or jumping** in the last third.

## Getting ready for whelping
- Set up a clean, warm, draft-free **whelping box** in a quiet room a week before due date so she can settle in.
- Know the **baseline resting temperature** of the female; a drop of ~1°C (1.5–2°F) below her normal (often to 99°F / 37.2°C) typically happens within 24 hours of labor.
- Keep the **vet's after-hours number** and the nearest emergency clinic's number handy.
- Have on hand: clean towels, unwaxed dental floss (to tie cords if needed), a digital thermometer, a scale to weigh puppies, a notepad.

## Stages of labor
**Stage 1 (preparation)**: restless, panting, nesting, appetite loss. Typically 6–12 hours. No visible straining yet.
**Stage 2 (active)**: visible contractions and puppies delivered, usually one every 30–60 minutes. Total stage 2 can last many hours for large litters.
**Stage 3 (placenta delivery)**: a placenta passes after each puppy. Retained placentas can cause postpartum infection.

The mother typically breaks the sac, chews the cord, and licks the puppy to stimulate breathing. Intervene only if she doesn't.

## Emergencies during labor — call or go to the vet immediately
- **Strong contractions for more than 30 minutes without producing a puppy**
- **Weak, intermittent contractions for more than 2 hours without a puppy**
- **More than 2 hours between puppies** when more are still expected
- A **green/black discharge** without a puppy following within about 15 minutes (placenta separating before delivery — oxygen to puppy is compromised)
- A puppy is **visible but stuck** and does not progress within a few minutes of gentle assistance
- The mother seems **exhausted, trembling, weak, collapsed, or unresponsive**
- Labor has not started **past day 68** of gestation
- **Foul-smelling, bloody, or pus-like discharge**
- Mother is **shivering, staggering, restless** after delivery — may be **eclampsia** (low blood calcium), an emergency in small breeds with large litters

## Normal postpartum and warning signs

### Normal
- Red-brown vaginal discharge for 1–3 weeks, tapering off
- Appetite up (may eat 2–3× maintenance while nursing)
- Tired but interested in puppies
- Gradual weight loss as litter grows

### Call the vet
- **Foul-smelling or heavy bloody discharge** after delivery
- **Fever** (above 103°F / 39.4°C)
- **No appetite** or obvious sickness
- **Swollen, hot, painful mammary gland** — mastitis
- **Not producing milk** or puppies not growing / constantly crying
- **Trembling, muscle twitching, restlessness, seizures in mother** — possible **eclampsia**, emergency
- **Retained placenta** (count placentas against number of puppies)

## Caring for newborns — quick basics
- Newborn puppies cannot regulate temperature; keep the whelping area **around 85–90°F (29–32°C)** the first week, then gradually lower.
- They nurse every 2–4 hours initially. **Weigh daily** — steady gain is the best early-warning sign.
- Umbilical cords usually dry and fall off within a week.
- Vet visit at 2–3 weeks for first exam + deworming schedule.
- Normal milestones: eyes open around 10–14 days, walking around 3 weeks, weaning starts around 4 weeks.

## About intentional breeding
Breeding healthy, well-tempered dogs responsibly requires pre-breeding health screening appropriate to the breed (hips, elbows, eyes, genetic panels, cardiac), veterinary-supervised timing, and planning for emergencies and puppy placement. If you're considering breeding for the first time, work with your veterinarian and an experienced breeder — complications can be costly and dangerous without preparation.
