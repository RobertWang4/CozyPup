## What it is
Dogs commonly pick up internal parasites — worms and single-celled organisms — from contaminated soil, feces, prey, fleas, mosquitoes, or mother's milk. Intestinal parasites can cause digestive problems, weight loss, anemia, and, in some cases, disease in humans.

## Common intestinal parasites
- **Roundworms (Toxocara, Toxascaris)** — very common in puppies; visible as spaghetti-like worms in vomit or stool.
- **Hookworms (Ancylostoma, Uncinaria)** — blood-feeders; can cause anemia, especially in young puppies.
- **Whipworms (Trichuris vulpis)** — live in the large intestine; may cause chronic diarrhea.
- **Tapeworms (Dipylidium, Taenia, Echinococcus)** — look like rice grains near the anus or in stool; many are picked up via fleas.
- **Giardia and Coccidia** — single-celled parasites that commonly cause diarrhea, especially in puppies and newly adopted dogs.

## Heartworm — a separate, serious threat
Heartworms (*Dirofilaria immitis*) are spread by mosquitoes and live in the heart and lungs. Infection can be fatal and is far easier to prevent than to treat. Year-round monthly preventive medication is standard practice across North America. Dogs are typically tested for heartworm yearly before a prescription is renewed.

## Zoonotic concern
Roundworm and hookworm eggs and larvae from dog feces can infect people — especially children — if ingested or absorbed through skin contact with contaminated soil. Picking up stool promptly and handwashing reduce this risk.

## Signs of parasites
- Visible worms in stool or vomit
- Diarrhea, sometimes with blood or mucus
- Weight loss despite a normal appetite, or a pot-bellied appearance in puppies
- Dull coat, low energy
- Scooting (sometimes, though anal gland issues are a more common cause)
- Coughing can be a sign of heartworm (not typical for intestinal worms)

Some parasites cause no obvious signs, which is why routine fecal testing matters.

## Preventive strategy
- **Puppies:** deworming usually begins at a few weeks of age and is repeated on a schedule set by the vet, because puppies often acquire worms from their mother.
- **Adult dogs:** yearly fecal testing; monthly parasite-preventive medication prescribed by the vet often covers heartworm plus several intestinal worms.
- **Fleas and ticks** spread certain parasites — keep flea and tick prevention current too.
- Pick up stool daily and wash hands after.

## When to see the vet
- You see worms or worm segments in stool, vomit, or around the anus.
- Persistent or bloody diarrhea, poor growth in a puppy, or unexplained weight loss.
- You missed a heartworm preventive dose by several weeks — the vet may want to retest before restarting.
- You adopted a new dog with unknown deworming history.

## What the vet may do
Diagnosis usually involves a fecal exam (flotation, direct smear, or antigen tests) and sometimes blood tests. Treatment depends on the specific parasite and is prescribed accordingly — different worms respond to different medications. Retesting after treatment confirms that the parasites have cleared.
