## What to expect
Spay and neuter are common surgeries, but they are still real abdominal or scrotal operations performed under general anesthesia. Most pets go home the same day or the next day and recover uneventfully at home over about **10–14 days**. Your job during that time is to keep the incision clean, keep the pet calm, and watch for problems.

## Day 1 (the day you come home)
- Your pet will likely be **groggy, wobbly, or sleepy** from anesthesia for the rest of the day. This is normal.
- Offer a **small amount of water and a small bland meal** in the evening, unless your vet said otherwise. Appetite often returns fully within 24 hours.
- Keep the pet in a **quiet, warm, confined area** away from stairs, other pets, and children.
- **Leave the e-collar on.** Even a sleepy pet will lick the incision.
- Give pain medication and any other prescribed medicines exactly as directed by your veterinarian — **do not give human pain relievers** (ibuprofen, acetaminophen, naproxen, aspirin); many are toxic to dogs and cats.

## Days 2–7
- **Check the incision once or twice daily** in good light. A thin line of mild redness and a small amount of bruising can be normal. Look for: new swelling, heat, discharge (especially pus or fresh blood), a gap in the edges, or missing sutures.
- **Keep the incision dry.** No bathing, swimming, beach, or letting the pet roll in wet grass. Do not apply ointments, creams, hydrogen peroxide, or alcohol unless your vet specifically said to.
- **Strict activity restriction:** leash walks only for dogs (short, on-leash bathroom breaks), no running, jumping, rough play, or stairs. Cats should stay indoors in a small room, ideally without high furniture to jump on and off of.
- **Keep the e-collar on at all times**, including during sleep and meals. Licking and chewing is the **number one cause of post-surgical complications** — one determined lick can open an incision that took the vet 20 minutes to close.
- Appetite, energy, and bathroom habits should be close to normal by day 3–4. Mild grogginess on day 1 that doesn't improve by day 2 warrants a call.

## Days 7–14
- Activity still restricted. Most clinics schedule a **recheck or suture/staple removal around 10–14 days** after surgery. Some use absorbable sutures under the skin that don't need removal — follow your clinic's instructions.
- The e-collar stays on **until the vet says the incision is fully healed**, not just until it "looks okay." Healing under the skin takes longer than the surface.
- After the vet clears your pet, return to normal activity **gradually** over several days, not all at once.

## Incision care checklist
Look for the incision to become **less red, less swollen, and drier** over time — not the other way around. A healthy incision:
- Has edges that are touching, not gapping
- Is not hot to the touch
- Has no discharge, or only a tiny bit of clear/pink fluid in the first day or two
- Is not increasing in size

## When to call the veterinarian
Call sooner rather than later if you see:
- **Bleeding** from the incision that doesn't stop, or a sudden gush of fluid
- The **incision opening** or sutures missing
- **Swelling, heat, or pus** at the incision, or a bad smell
- **Vomiting, refusing food for more than 24 hours**, ongoing diarrhea, or collapse
- **Pain that isn't controlled** by the prescribed medication (whining, restlessness, trembling, hiding)
- Pale gums, labored breathing, or a swollen belly — these can be emergencies
- Your pet licking or chewing the incision despite the e-collar
