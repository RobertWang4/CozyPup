## What it is
Dogs commonly swallow things they shouldn't — bones, rawhide pieces, toys, socks, corn cobs, fishhooks, needles, string, balls, rocks, clothing, pieces of plastic, and coins. The object may pass harmlessly, or it may lodge in the esophagus, stomach, or intestines and cause a medical emergency.

## Possible consequences
- **Esophageal obstruction** — object stuck in the swallowing tube between mouth and stomach.
- **Gastric obstruction or irritation** — object stuck in the stomach.
- **Intestinal obstruction** — object lodged in the small or large intestine; can lead to perforation and sepsis.
- **Linear foreign body** (string, ribbon, fabric) — especially dangerous because it can saw through the intestinal wall.
- **Poisoning** — if the swallowed item is toxic (chocolate, raisins, xylitol, medications, household chemicals).

## Signs to watch for
- Sudden excessive drooling, gagging, or repeated attempts to swallow
- Vomiting, especially shortly after eating or drinking
- Loss of appetite, lethargy
- Painful, distended, or tense belly; a hunched posture
- Straining to defecate, diarrhea, or passing no stool at all
- Visible string or fabric under the tongue — **do not pull on it**

## When to seek veterinary care
**Call a veterinarian right away** if:
- You saw or strongly suspect your dog swallowed a foreign object.
- The swallowed item is sharp, long, string-like, a battery, a magnet, a medication, or a toxic food.
- Your dog is vomiting, drooling excessively, or in obvious distress.
- The belly looks bloated or is painful to touch.

This is one of the scenarios where waiting can make the difference between a straightforward removal and major surgery.

## What the vet may do
Assessment typically includes a physical exam and imaging (X-rays, sometimes with contrast, or ultrasound). Depending on where the object is and what it is, treatment may involve inducing vomiting, endoscopic retrieval (using a flexible scope through the mouth), or abdominal surgery. Supportive care for dehydration, pain, and infection is often needed.

## What to do at home
- Note what you think was swallowed and when, and bring packaging or a similar item to the clinic.
- **Do not try to induce vomiting unless a veterinarian tells you to.** For sharp objects, corrosives, and unstable dogs, vomiting can cause serious harm.
- Do not feed or give water until the vet has examined the dog.
- Keep the dog calm and minimize movement on the way to the clinic.

## Prevention
- Store household items, medications, and children's toys out of reach.
- Choose toys sized appropriately for your dog; retire them when they get chewed up.
- Skip cooked bones and fatty scraps; supervise bully sticks and rawhide.
- Secure trash cans and laundry hampers.
