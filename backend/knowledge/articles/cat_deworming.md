## What it is
Cats can pick up internal parasites from the environment, prey, fleas, or from their mother. Both outdoor and strictly indoor cats can be affected, though outdoor cats and hunters have more exposure.

## Common intestinal parasites in cats
- **Roundworms (Toxocara cati)** — common in kittens; may be visible as spaghetti-like worms in vomit or stool.
- **Hookworms** — blood-feeders; can cause anemia, especially in kittens.
- **Tapeworms (Dipylidium caninum, Taenia)** — rice-grain-like segments near the anus or in stool; usually picked up through fleas or prey.
- **Giardia** — a single-celled parasite that causes diarrhea.
- **Coccidia** — single-celled parasites; common in kittens with diarrhea.
- **Tritrichomonas foetus** — causes chronic diarrhea, especially in young cats from multi-cat environments.

## Heartworm in cats — a separate concern
Heartworms are spread by mosquitoes. Cats are not the preferred host, so infection numbers are lower, but heartworm disease in cats can still cause severe respiratory disease or sudden death. Prevention is far easier than treatment; year-round preventives are standard in heartworm-endemic areas, even for indoor-only cats (mosquitoes get inside).

## Zoonotic concern
Roundworm and hookworm larvae from cat feces can infect people — especially children — through contact with contaminated soil or litter. Good litter-box hygiene and handwashing reduce the risk.

## Signs of parasites
- Visible worms or rice-like segments in stool, vomit, or near the anus
- Diarrhea (sometimes with blood or mucus), or soft stool that won't resolve
- Weight loss despite appetite, or a pot-bellied kitten
- Dull coat, low energy
- Vomiting
- Coughing or breathing changes can be a sign of heartworm (not typical for intestinal worms)

Some cats show no signs, which is why routine fecal testing matters.

## Preventive strategy
- **Kittens** are typically dewormed starting at a few weeks of age and repeated on a schedule set by the vet, because they often acquire worms from the queen.
- **Adult cats** benefit from yearly fecal testing and a veterinarian-prescribed year-round parasite preventive. Many monthly topical or oral products cover several parasites at once.
- **Flea control year-round** — fleas carry tapeworms and other parasites.
- Keep litter boxes clean, scoop daily, and wash hands afterward.
- If your cat hunts or goes outdoors, the vet may deworm more frequently.

## When to see the vet
- You see worms or worm segments in stool, around the anus, or in vomit
- Persistent or bloody diarrhea, vomiting, poor growth in a kitten, weight loss
- Your cat coughs, wheezes, or suddenly becomes very short of breath
- You missed a preventive dose; the vet may want to test before restarting
- You adopted a new cat with unknown history

## What the vet may do
Diagnosis usually involves a fecal exam (flotation, direct smear, antigen tests, or PCR) and sometimes blood tests. The treatment depends on the specific parasite — different parasites respond to different medications. A retest after treatment confirms success.
