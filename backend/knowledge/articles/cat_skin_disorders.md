## What it is
Dermatitis means inflammation of the skin. It is a general label used until a specific cause is identified. Skin problems in cats can come from external irritants, allergens, parasites, bacterial or fungal infection, trauma, or from internal conditions that show up on the skin.

Cats often hide early skin problems because they groom discreetly. By the time owners notice, there may already be bald patches, scabs, or over-grooming sores.

## How skin disease shows up in cats
- **Over-grooming** or excessive licking of certain areas (belly, inner thighs, flanks, paws)
- **Hair loss (alopecia)**, patchy or symmetrical
- **Scabs and crusts**, often on the neck, back, and around the ears — this is a common presentation called miliary dermatitis
- **Redness, bumps, or pimples**
- **Itching, scratching, and head-shaking**
- **Ear problems** — wax, dark discharge, head-shaking (often tied to allergies or ear mites)
- **Odor** or greasy coat
- **Weight change, drinking/urinating changes** — these can signal a hormonal cause

## Common causes
- **Fleas and flea allergy** — the single most common cause of skin problems in cats; signs can be intense even with only a few fleas.
- **Other parasites** — ear mites, *Cheyletiella* (walking dandruff), lice, *Demodex*.
- **Environmental or food allergies** — often year-round itching, sometimes with digestive signs.
- **Bacterial and yeast infections** — usually secondary to another underlying cause.
- **Ringworm (dermatophytosis)** — a fungus; contagious to people and other pets. Very common source of patchy hair loss, especially in kittens.
- **Abscesses** from bite wounds in cats that fight outdoors.

## What to note for the vet
- Where on the body the problem is, and how it started
- Whether other pets or people in the home have skin or hair problems
- Flea and tick prevention status
- Recent changes: diet, new products, new pets, travel
- Over-grooming patterns — many cats over-groom privately
- Any other signs — weight changes, appetite changes, thirst and urination changes

## When to see the vet
- Hair loss, sores, or scabs appearing or spreading
- Intense or new itching, over-grooming, or head-shaking
- Signs of infection — pus, bad smell, open wounds
- A lump or bite wound, especially in a cat that goes outside
- Anyone in the home develops skin rashes or itching — some conditions (ringworm, some mange) can spread to people

## What the vet may do
Evaluation typically includes history, a full skin exam, and targeted diagnostics: skin scrapings for mites, tape or impression cytology for yeast and bacteria, fungal culture for ringworm, and sometimes bloodwork for hormonal causes or allergy testing. Treatment addresses both the underlying cause and any secondary infection or inflammation.

## Home care guidance
- Keep **year-round flea prevention** current — many feline skin problems trace back to fleas, even in "indoor-only" cats.
- Bathe with a shampoo recommended for your cat's specific problem; avoid human shampoos.
- If your cat is over-grooming raw spots, an e-collar (and veterinary attention to the cause) can prevent further damage.
- **Do not apply human topical creams** (including hydrocortisone or anti-fungals) without veterinary approval — cats are especially sensitive, and some products are toxic if licked.
