## What it is
Chronic kidney disease (CKD) is long-term, progressive loss of kidney function. It is one of the most common serious diseases in older cats — up to about **35% of cats over age 5–6** develop some degree of CKD. Damaged kidney tissue does not grow back, but with early detection and steady management, cats can live comfortably for months to years, even with very little remaining kidney function.

CKD is different from a sudden (acute) kidney injury caused by toxins like lily ingestion or antifreeze. Both damage the kidneys, but chronic disease builds slowly over months or years.

## Stages, briefly
Veterinarians classify CKD into 4 stages based on blood and urine tests:
- **Stage I / II** — kidneys are damaged but the cat often has no outward signs. This is when treatment is most effective, and it is usually only caught on routine senior bloodwork.
- **Stage III** — waste products build up in the blood and the cat starts to feel unwell.
- **Stage IV** — end-stage; the cat is visibly sick and supportive care becomes the focus.

High blood pressure and protein loss in the urine are tracked separately because both accelerate kidney damage.

## Signs to watch for
Early signs are subtle. Later signs get harder to miss:
- **Drinking more water and peeing more** (often the very first clue)
- Larger, paler clumps in the litter box
- Gradual **weight loss** despite normal or reduced appetite
- Poor coat, looking "unkempt"
- Loss of appetite, picky eating
- Vomiting, sometimes daily or in bouts
- Mouth sores, bad breath (ammonia-like)
- Lethargy, hiding more, sleeping more
- Dehydration (skin tents, eyes look sunken)
- Sudden blindness — can indicate severe high blood pressure from CKD

## Questions to think about before seeing the vet
- How much water does the cat drink in 24 hours? Has it changed?
- Litter box: more clumps, bigger clumps, or both?
- Weight trend over the last 6–12 months
- Any vomiting — how often, when relative to meals?
- Appetite changes, preference for wet over dry food
- Any lilies, NSAIDs, or antifreeze possibly accessed?

## When to seek veterinary care
Book a visit when you see increased thirst/urination in a middle-aged or older cat — this is the single most actionable early sign. Seek care **promptly** (within a day or two) for ongoing vomiting, rapid weight loss, refusal to eat, or weakness. Seek **urgent/emergency** care for sudden blindness, collapse, seizures, or a cat that has stopped eating and drinking entirely.

## What the vet may do
- Physical exam, weight and body-condition scoring, hydration check
- Blood tests (kidney values including SDMA and creatinine, electrolytes, phosphorus, red blood cell count)
- Urine tests — concentration, protein, and a check for urinary tract infection
- Blood pressure measurement
- Imaging (ultrasound or x-ray) when indicated
- A personalized plan: kidney-friendly diet, management of blood pressure and urine protein, phosphorus management, anti-nausea support, treatment of any concurrent urinary infection
- Teaching owners to give subcutaneous fluids at home in later stages

## Home care guidance
- **Never restrict water.** Fresh water should always be available; many cats drink more from fountains or multiple bowls.
- Prioritize **wet food** over dry where the cat will accept it — extra moisture helps.
- Feed a **veterinarian-recommended kidney diet** when prescribed. Transition slowly over 1–2 weeks so the cat does not reject it.
- Weigh the cat monthly at home — a shrinking cat is often a warning.
- Keep rechecks on schedule: roughly every 6–12 months in early stages, every 3–6 months in stage 2–3, and every 1–3 months in stage 3–4.
- Keep lilies completely out of the household — even pollen or vase water can trigger acute kidney failure in cats.
- Never give human pain relievers (ibuprofen, acetaminophen, naproxen) — any of these can worsen kidney damage or be fatal.
