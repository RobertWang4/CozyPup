## What it is
Cats sometimes swallow objects they shouldn't — and the consequences can be serious. **Strings, yarn, ribbon, dental floss, tinsel, rubber bands, and hair ties are especially dangerous in cats** because these "linear foreign bodies" can catch at the base of the tongue or in the intestine and saw through the intestinal wall as the gut tries to push them along. Other hazards include small toys, bones, needles, tinfoil, plastic, fishing tackle, and houseplants.

## Possible consequences
- **Esophageal obstruction** — object lodged in the swallowing tube
- **Gastric irritation or obstruction** — object stuck in the stomach
- **Intestinal obstruction** — can lead to perforation, peritonitis, and sepsis
- **Linear foreign body** — particularly severe; may require surgery
- **Poisoning** — if the swallowed item is toxic (lilies, Easter lilies are deadly to cats; also some human medications, cleaners)

## Signs to watch for
- Sudden excessive drooling, gagging, or repeated swallowing
- Vomiting, especially shortly after eating or drinking
- Refusing food, hiding, lethargy
- Painful, tense, or distended belly; a hunched posture
- Straining in the litter box, decreased or absent stool
- **Visible string or thread under the tongue** — **do not pull on it**; it may be attached inside

## When to seek veterinary care
**Call a veterinarian immediately** if:
- You saw or strongly suspect your cat swallowed any string-like object, a needle, a battery, a magnet, a medication, or a toxic plant or substance
- Your cat is repeatedly vomiting, drooling, or in obvious distress
- There is a string hanging from the mouth or anus
- The belly looks bloated or the cat cries when it is touched
- The cat has not eaten or used the litter box normally for more than 24 hours

Lilies (including Easter lily, tiger lily, daylily, Asiatic lily) are **life-threatening** to cats even in small amounts — if you suspect ingestion, this is an emergency.

## What the vet may do
Evaluation usually includes a history, a careful physical exam including looking under the tongue, and imaging (X-rays, sometimes with contrast, or ultrasound). Treatment depends on where the object is and what it is — options include endoscopic retrieval through the mouth, abdominal surgery, and supportive care for dehydration, pain, and infection.

## What to do at home
- Note what was swallowed and when; bring packaging or a similar item
- **Do not try to induce vomiting** — it can be harmful and is particularly risky for sharp or linear foreign bodies
- Do not pull on any visible string
- Do not offer food or water until the vet says it is safe
- Keep the cat calm in a carrier for transport

## Prevention
- Store sewing kits, elastic bands, hair ties, floss, earbuds, and children's toys out of reach
- Never let a cat play unsupervised with string, yarn, ribbon, or tinsel
- Secure trash cans and remove access to houseplants (especially lilies, for cats)
- Choose toys too large to be swallowed; discard toys as they wear out
