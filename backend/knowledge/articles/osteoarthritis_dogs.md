## What it is
Osteoarthritis — also called degenerative joint disease — is the gradual breakdown of cartilage in the joints, along with low-grade inflammation and the formation of bony outgrowths around the joint. Over time, cartilage thins, fluid builds up in the joint, the joint membrane thickens, and the joint becomes stiff and painful.

It is one of the most common causes of chronic pain in dogs, especially seniors and large breeds. Cats also develop osteoarthritis frequently but tend to hide it better.

## Why it happens
- **Developmental joint disease** — hip dysplasia, elbow dysplasia, and cruciate ligament disease
- **Previous joint injury** — fractures, ligament tears, dislocations
- **Body weight** — excess weight dramatically accelerates wear on joints
- **Age** — cartilage health declines with age in virtually all dogs
- **Joint infection or immune-mediated joint disease** (less common)

Large and giant breeds, active working dogs, and dogs with juvenile orthopedic problems are the most likely to develop arthritis early.

## Signs to watch for
Dogs rarely "cry" from arthritis — the signs are subtle and gradual:
- Reluctance to go up or down stairs
- Hesitation to jump onto the couch or into the car
- **Stiff, slow to rise** after resting, especially in the morning or after cold weather
- Shortened stride, "bunny-hopping" with the hind legs
- Lameness that comes and goes — often worse after heavy exercise, better after warming up
- Obvious limping on one leg
- Licking a specific joint repeatedly
- Muscle loss over the hips or shoulders
- Irritability or avoiding being touched on the back/hips
- Loss of interest in long walks or play

## Questions to think about before seeing the vet
- Which leg(s) look affected, and is it worse at certain times of day?
- Better or worse with cold weather?
- How is the dog on hard floors vs. rugs?
- Has weight changed recently?
- Any recent injuries or sudden onset events?
- Has appetite or overall energy changed?

## When to seek veterinary care
Book a routine visit when you see mild, gradual changes in mobility — treating arthritis **early** makes a large difference in long-term comfort.

Seek care **sooner** (within a day or two) for:
- Sudden onset severe lameness, especially non-weight-bearing
- A leg held up that is obviously swollen, hot, or painful
- A dog that is very reluctant to move at all
- Signs that follow a known injury (fall, car accident, rough play)

A sudden severe lameness is not "just arthritis" — it could be a torn ligament, fracture, bone infection, or tumor, and deserves prompt evaluation.

## What the vet may do
- Physical exam including gait observation, joint range-of-motion testing, and muscle assessment
- X-rays to look at specific joints, spine, and hips
- Blood work when starting long-term anti-inflammatory medication
- A **multimodal plan** that combines weight management, exercise modification, anti-inflammatory and pain-control medications, joint supplements, physical rehabilitation, and sometimes surgery (arthroscopy, joint stabilization, fusion, or replacement) for specific problems

Specific drugs and doses are always prescribed by the veterinarian based on the individual dog, including age, kidney and liver function, and other medications.

## Home care guidance
- **Keep the dog lean.** This is the single most effective thing an owner can do for arthritic joints. Ask the veterinarian for a target weight.
- **Regular, low-impact exercise** — steady leash walks and gentle swimming are better than weekend "warrior" bursts of running and fetching.
- **Non-slip rugs or runners** on hardwood and tile floors. Slipping is painful and frightening for an arthritic dog.
- **Ramps instead of jumps** in and out of cars or onto beds/couches.
- **Orthopedic bed** — a well-padded, supportive bed in a warm spot.
- Keep nails trimmed — long nails change posture and worsen joint pain.
- Consider physical therapy, underwater treadmill, or acupuncture if available in your area.
- Ask the veterinarian about joint supplements (omega-3s, glucosamine/chondroitin, green-lipped mussel) — evidence varies, but several are safe and commonly recommended.
- **Never give human pain relievers** — ibuprofen, naproxen, and acetaminophen can cause kidney failure, stomach ulcers, or death in dogs.

## A note on cats
Arthritis is under-recognized in cats because they hide pain well. Warning signs include hesitation to jump, missing a landing, urinating or defecating next to rather than in a tall litter box, reduced grooming over the lower back, and increased time sleeping. If your cat shows these changes, ask the veterinarian about feline-specific pain management.
