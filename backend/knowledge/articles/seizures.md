## What a seizure looks like
A seizure is a sudden, uncontrolled burst of abnormal electrical activity in the brain. Common presentations in dogs and cats:

- **Generalized (grand mal):** sudden collapse, stiff body, paddling legs, chewing motions, drooling or foaming at the mouth, loss of bladder/bowel control, unresponsive. Usually lasts **1–2 minutes**.
- **Focal (partial):** twitching of one part of the body, facial tic, chomping, staring, or unusual behavior. The pet may still be partly aware.
- **Pre-seizure (aura):** restless, clingy, hiding, pacing, vomiting, or staring — minutes to hours before.
- **Post-seizure (postictal):** disorientation, wobbliness, temporary blindness, thirst, hunger, or exhaustion, lasting minutes to hours.

## Common causes
- **Idiopathic epilepsy** — usually starts in young/middle-aged dogs with no identifiable cause; treatable with long-term medication.
- **Structural brain disease** — tumor, inflammation (meningitis, encephalitis), stroke, trauma, developmental malformations.
- **Toxins** — chocolate, xylitol, human medications, marijuana, certain rodenticides, moldy food, pesticides, some human recreational drugs.
- **Metabolic** — low blood sugar (common in toy-breed puppies and diabetic pets), liver disease, kidney disease, electrolyte disturbances, severe heatstroke.
- **Infections** — distemper in dogs, some viral/fungal/parasitic diseases.
- **Nutritional** — severe thiamine deficiency (rare).

## What to do during a seizure
1. **Stay calm. Note the time** — seizures that last longer than 3 minutes, or cluster seizures, are emergencies.
2. **Move furniture, corners, stairs, and other hazards away.** Don't try to hold the pet down.
3. **Keep hands away from the mouth.** Pets will not swallow their tongue; you will get bitten.
4. **Dim the lights** and reduce noise.
5. **Do not throw water** or put anything in the mouth.
6. **Record video** if you can — this is extremely helpful for diagnosis.
7. After it stops, let the pet rest in a safe, quiet spot while they come around.

## Emergency — go now
Call your vet or an emergency clinic **immediately** if:
- A single seizure lasts **more than 3 minutes** (status epilepticus — life-threatening)
- **More than one seizure in 24 hours** (cluster seizures)
- First-ever seizure, **especially in a cat** (seizures are less common in cats and often have a serious cause)
- Seizure after possible toxin exposure (chocolate, xylitol, human meds, marijuana, rodenticide)
- Pet does not come around to normal within an hour
- Seizure in a very young puppy (possible low blood sugar) or diabetic pet (treat under the tongue with a small amount of honey or corn syrup and go immediately)
- Seizure after head trauma, or with fever, or with other neurologic signs (wobbling, circling, blindness)

## Other neurologic warning signs
These also warrant a veterinary visit even without a visible seizure:
- **Walking in circles** or pushing the head against walls
- **Disorientation**, forgetting familiar people or places (especially in older pets)
- **Sudden paralysis or dragging a limb**
- **Loss of balance**, head tilt, rapid eye movement
- **Sudden blindness**
- Change in behavior or personality

## What the vet may do
The workup depends on age and presentation and can include: physical + neurologic exam, bloodwork (including liver, kidney, glucose, electrolytes, thyroid), urinalysis, toxin screening, blood pressure, and — when indicated — MRI and spinal fluid analysis. Treatment targets the underlying cause; for idiopathic epilepsy, daily anti-seizure medication usually controls the seizures well.

## Life with an epileptic pet
- Keep a **seizure log**: date, time, duration, what happened before/after, any missed medication doses.
- **Give medications exactly as prescribed** — missed doses can trigger seizures. Don't stop abruptly.
- **Follow-up bloodwork** on schedule to monitor drug levels and organ function.
- Reduce triggers where known: stress, overheating, some flashing lights.
- Microchip and ID — a disoriented pet may bolt post-seizure.
