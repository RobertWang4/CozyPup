## What this covers
This article is about **wounds** in dogs and cats — cuts and lacerations, puncture wounds, bite wounds, and abscesses that form when bite wounds become infected. For heavy bleeding or major trauma, treat it as an emergency and go straight to a veterinary hospital; the notes below are for follow-up thinking once the pet is stable.

## Why bite wounds deserve special attention
Cat and dog teeth drive bacteria deep into the tissue through a small puncture, and the skin often seals over before the deeper tissue has cleared. A wound that looks like a single small scab on the outside can be hiding:
- A **pocket of infection (abscess)** developing under the skin over the next 2–5 days
- Torn muscle or fascia
- A **cracked rib or damaged organ** if the bite was on the chest or belly
- A bite on a joint capsule, which is an orthopedic emergency

Cat bites in particular are deep, narrow punctures and have a very high rate of infection; they often need antibiotics even when they look minor.

## Signs a wound is becoming a problem
- **Swelling** that appears or grows 2–5 days after a bite or scratch
- A warm, soft lump under the skin — often a forming abscess
- **Pain** disproportionate to how small the wound looks
- **Discharge** — clear, yellow, green, bloody, or foul-smelling
- **Redness** spreading beyond the wound edges
- Fever, lethargy, loss of appetite
- Lameness if a limb is involved
- The pet licking or chewing at one spot repeatedly
- An abscess that suddenly bursts and drains pus — surprisingly, this often makes the pet feel better, but still needs veterinary follow-up

## Questions to think about before seeing the vet
- When did the injury happen?
- Was the other animal known, and is its vaccine (especially rabies) status known?
- How deep does the wound look? Any punctures you can see?
- Is the pet using the limb/area normally?
- Any new swelling, pain, or discharge since the injury?
- Current vaccination status — is rabies up to date?

## When to seek veterinary care
Seek **prompt veterinary care (same day)** for:
- Any bite wound, even if small — especially from a cat, a wild animal, or an unknown animal
- Puncture wounds on the chest, belly, neck, or over a joint
- Wounds that are bleeding heavily or won't stop oozing with 5–10 minutes of firm pressure
- Wounds longer than about a half inch (1–2 cm) or that gape open
- Any wound with visible muscle, bone, or fat
- Burns beyond a small superficial area
- Wounds contaminated with dirt, oil, gravel, or road debris
- Wounds in very young, very old, or immunocompromised pets

Seek **emergency care** for:
- Gunshot wounds, wounds from being hit by a car, or large degloving injuries where skin has been sheared off
- Wounds on the eye, mouth, or throat
- An animal in shock — pale gums, rapid breathing, collapse
- A swelling that is rapidly growing, very hot, or producing foul-smelling drainage
- Possible rabies exposure (bite from wildlife or an unknown animal), especially if the pet is not up to date on rabies vaccination — this is a public-health issue as well

## What the vet may do
- Assess the pet for shock, check vital signs, and look for deeper injuries that may not be obvious
- Sedate for a thorough exam of the full extent of the wound — often much bigger underneath than it appears
- Clip the fur around the wound and **irrigate** it with sterile fluid to flush out bacteria and debris
- Remove dead or heavily contaminated tissue
- Decide between closing the wound (sutures, staples, or glue) vs. leaving it open to drain — older, contaminated, or infected wounds are often left open or closed with a drain
- Place a **drain** if there is dead space where fluid could collect
- Prescribe antibiotics matched to the likely bacteria (for bite wounds, often Pasteurella and anaerobes) and pain medication
- For abscesses: lance and drain, flush, sometimes place a drain, and follow up with antibiotics
- Bandaging, with instructions for changes and monitoring at home
- Update tetanus or rabies vaccination if indicated, and report bites per local public-health rules

## Home care guidance
**First aid before you can get to the vet:**
- Muzzle a painful pet carefully if needed for safety — even friendly animals bite when hurt
- Apply firm, direct pressure with a clean cloth to stop bleeding
- Rinse minor wounds with clean tap water or saline — do **not** use hydrogen peroxide, alcohol, or iodine straight from the bottle on open wounds; these damage tissue and slow healing
- Cover with a clean, dry cloth or gauze and head to the clinic

**After a vet visit:**
- Follow the prescribed antibiotic and pain medication schedule exactly; finish the full course
- Keep the pet from licking the wound — an Elizabethan collar ("cone") is almost always necessary
- Check the wound at least twice a day for swelling, redness, discharge, or opening up
- Keep the wound dry unless instructed otherwise; follow specific bandage-change instructions
- Limit activity per the vet's instructions to allow tissue to heal
- Return for recheck and suture removal on schedule

**Do not:**
- Apply human creams, ointments, or essential oils to a wound without veterinary guidance — many are toxic if licked
- Cover a wound with household adhesive tape directly on skin or fur
- Try to drain an abscess yourself at home
- Skip antibiotics once the pet "looks better" — infections regrow
