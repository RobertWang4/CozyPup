## What it is
Diabetes mellitus is a long-term disorder in which the body cannot properly control blood sugar (glucose). It happens when the pancreas does not make enough insulin, or when the body's cells stop responding normally to insulin. Both dogs and cats can develop diabetes, though the underlying patterns differ slightly: most diabetic **dogs** need lifelong insulin from the time of diagnosis, while a meaningful fraction of diabetic **cats** can go into remission if treated early and aggressively, particularly with diet changes and weight loss.

Diabetes is not the same as a pet "having a sweet tooth." It is a serious endocrine disease that, untreated, leads to a life-threatening complication called diabetic ketoacidosis.

## Who gets it
**Dogs:**
- Usually middle-aged or older
- Females affected about twice as often as males
- Overrepresented breeds include Miniature Poodle, Dachshund, Schnauzer, Cairn Terrier, Beagle (any breed can be affected)
- Obesity, long-term steroid treatment, recurrent pancreatitis, and intact females (hormones from heat cycles) raise risk

**Cats:**
- Usually older, overweight, and often male
- Overrepresented breeds include Burmese
- Obesity and low activity are major risk factors

## Signs to watch for
- **Drinking a lot more water** and **urinating a lot more** (often the first sign)
- Peeing in the house, or larger/heavier clumps in the litter box
- **Eating more but losing weight**
- Cloudy eyes / sudden cataracts (common in diabetic dogs)
- Poor coat quality
- Recurrent skin or bladder infections that don't resolve normally
- In cats: a "plantigrade" stance — walking on the hocks of the hind legs, from diabetic nerve damage
- Late/severe signs (emergency): vomiting, weakness, rapid breathing, sweet/acetone breath, collapse — possible **diabetic ketoacidosis**

## Questions to think about before seeing the vet
- How much water does the pet drink per day? Has it clearly increased?
- Weight trend over the last 3–6 months
- Is the pet eating more, less, or the same?
- Any recent steroid medications?
- For intact females: when was the last heat cycle?
- Any new cloudiness of the eyes?

## When to seek veterinary care
Book a visit **soon** (within a week) for increased thirst and urination with weight loss — this combination strongly points to diabetes or other endocrine disease and should not be watched for long.

Seek **emergency care immediately** for a diabetic pet with vomiting, refusal to eat, weakness, collapse, staggering after an insulin dose, or seizures. Very low blood sugar (hypoglycemia) from too much insulin or a missed meal is a true emergency.

## What the vet may do
- Confirm diagnosis with blood and urine tests showing persistently high glucose
- Check for complications: urinary infection, pancreatitis, Cushing's disease, ketoacidosis
- Start insulin therapy, with the dose and schedule tailored to the individual pet
- Teach the owner how to store insulin, draw it up, and inject it under the skin
- Recommend a consistent **diet and meal schedule** timed with insulin
- Recommend **spaying** an intact diabetic female dog — heat cycle hormones make diabetes very hard to control
- Schedule follow-up blood glucose checks (often including at-home monitoring) to fine-tune the dose over weeks to months

Specific insulin dose decisions are always made by the veterinarian and should never be adjusted at home without guidance.

## Home care guidance
- Give insulin injections and meals on the **same schedule every day**. Consistency is more important than clock-perfect timing.
- **Never double an insulin dose** if you think you missed one; call the clinic.
- If your pet vomits, refuses to eat, or seems unsteady, call the veterinarian **before** giving the next insulin dose. In many cases the dose should be reduced or skipped.
- Keep a small source of sugar (honey, corn syrup, glucose gel) at home. If your pet becomes weak, staggering, or unresponsive after insulin, rub a small amount on the gums and head to the emergency vet.
- Weigh regularly. Weight changes affect insulin requirements.
- For diabetic cats, a **low-carbohydrate diet** and weight loss can sometimes push the disease into remission; follow the veterinarian's specific diet plan.
- Keep up with dental, vaccine, and parasite care — diabetic pets are more infection-prone.
