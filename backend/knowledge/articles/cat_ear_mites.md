## What it is
Ear mites (*Otodectes cynotis*) are tiny parasites that live in the external ear canal. They are **extremely common in cats**, especially kittens, cats from shelters, outdoor cats, and multi-cat homes. Ear mites are highly contagious between animals in close contact, and they can also be found on the body, not only in the ears.

## Signs to watch for
- **Head shaking** and scratching at the ears
- **Ears held low** or tilting the head
- **Dark, crumbly, coffee-ground-like debris** in the ear canal
- **Ear odor** and visible redness
- **Scratching around the head and neck** severe enough to cause sores and scabs
- In severe cases, pus, bleeding, or a torn eardrum

Itching can be intense. Secondary bacterial or yeast infections often develop on top of the mite infestation. In multi-cat households, expect more than one cat to be affected even if only one shows signs.

## When to see the vet
- Any new or worsening ear scratching, head shaking, or ear discharge
- Dark debris deep in the ear canal
- Your cat cries or pulls away when you touch the ear
- The ear flap is swollen or has open sores
- You have multiple cats and one is showing ear signs — all should be checked

From the outside, ear mites look similar to bacterial and yeast ear infections, and the treatments differ, so a veterinary diagnosis matters.

## What the vet may do
The veterinarian typically looks in the ear with an otoscope, collects a sample of debris, and examines it under a microscope to identify mites. The vet will also check for secondary infection and for an intact eardrum, which affects which products are safe to use.

Treatment usually involves:
- **Cleaning the ear canal** to remove debris
- **A parasiticide** applied in the ears, as a spot-on on the body, or given orally — the choice depends on the cat
- **Treating secondary infections** if present
- **Treating all pets in the home** that have been in contact

Follow-up rechecks confirm the mites are gone.

## Home care guidance
- Finish the **entire course of treatment** even if the cat looks better early — mites have a life cycle, and stopping early allows them to return.
- Wash bedding, soft toys, and grooming tools; vacuum favorite resting spots.
- **Do not use home remedies, mineral oil, olive oil, human ear drops, or generic "cleaners" without veterinary guidance** — some are painful, damage the eardrum, or are toxic to cats.
- Keep year-round parasite prevention; some monthly feline products also help cover ear mites.
