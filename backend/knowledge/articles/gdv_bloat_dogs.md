## What it is
Bloat — also called gastric dilatation and volvulus (GDV) — is a life-threatening emergency in dogs. The stomach fills with gas and fluid and then twists along its axis, trapping its contents and cutting off blood flow to the stomach and spleen. Without rapid veterinary care, many dogs die within hours. Approximately 25%–30% of affected dogs die even with treatment.

GDV is different from simple gas or indigestion. Once the stomach has twisted, nothing the owner can do at home will fix it — only a veterinary hospital can.

## Which dogs are at highest risk
GDV primarily affects **large, deep-chested dogs**. Commonly affected breeds include:
- Great Dane, Saint Bernard, Irish Setter, Weimaraner
- Doberman Pinscher, German Shepherd, Standard Poodle, Gordon Setter
- Basset Hound (deep chest despite small size)

Other risk factors:
- Lean body type
- Older age (risk rises with age)
- Anxious, aggressive, or fearful temperament
- Eating one large meal per day
- Eating quickly
- Dry-kibble-only diet
- A parent, sibling, or offspring with a history of bloat

## Signs to watch for
Bloat usually comes on quickly, often after a large meal followed by activity. Classic signs:
- A swollen, tight, drum-like belly
- Repeated **unproductive retching** — the dog looks like it is trying to vomit but nothing (or only foam/saliva) comes up
- Excessive drooling
- Restlessness, pacing, unable to get comfortable
- Rapid or labored breathing
- Obvious abdominal pain — hunched posture, whining when belly is touched
- Pale gums, rapid weak pulse, collapse (signs of shock in later stages)

## When to seek veterinary care — this is always an emergency
Go to an emergency veterinary hospital **immediately** — not "first thing in the morning" — if your dog shows any combination of the above, especially:
- A distended, tense abdomen
- Non-productive retching
- Signs of shock (pale gums, collapse, fast weak pulse)

Minutes matter. Call the clinic while you are on your way so they can prepare. Do not try to give food, water, oil, or anti-gas products — these will not untwist the stomach and can delay real care.

## What the vet may do
- Stabilize the dog for shock with IV fluids and oxygen
- Decompress the stomach by passing a tube through the mouth, or by inserting a large needle through the body wall if the twist prevents tube passage
- X-rays to confirm the stomach has rotated
- Surgery (almost always needed) to untwist the stomach, assess the stomach wall and spleen for dead tissue, remove damaged tissue, and **tack the stomach to the body wall** (a procedure called gastropexy) so it cannot twist again
- Ongoing monitoring for complications including abnormal heart rhythms, infection, peritonitis, and clotting problems

## Home care and prevention
There is no safe home treatment once GDV has started. Prevention is the focus:
- For high-risk breeds, ask the veterinarian about **prophylactic gastropexy**, often done at the same time as spay/neuter. A tacked stomach can still bloat but is far less likely to twist.
- Feed **two or three smaller meals per day**, not one large meal.
- Avoid vigorous exercise for at least an hour before and after meals.
- Discourage gulping — slow-feeder bowls can help.
- Don't let the dog drink large volumes of water right after heavy exercise.
- Reduce stress around mealtimes where possible.
- If one related dog has had GDV, be especially vigilant with littermates and offspring.

Know your nearest 24-hour emergency veterinary hospital before you need it. For a large, deep-chested dog, this single piece of information may save its life.
